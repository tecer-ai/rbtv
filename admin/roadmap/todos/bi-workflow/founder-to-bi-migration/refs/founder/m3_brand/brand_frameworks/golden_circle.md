# Golden Circle

**Purpose:** Task-based guide for using Simon Sinek's Golden Circle framework to define brand purpose through three concentric layers — Why, How, What — starting from the inside out, in M3 Brand Step 4.

**Context:**
Read `brand_process.md` before starting. Execute in M3 Brand Step 4: Define Purpose. MUST complete Brand Prism first. Uses Brand Prism culture facet and JTBD emotional jobs as primary inputs.

---

## Framework Overview

The Golden Circle is a purpose-definition model introduced by Simon Sinek. It structures brand identity around three concentric circles: **Why** (the purpose, cause, or belief that drives the organisation), **How** (the differentiated approach, values, or process through which the organisation delivers on its purpose), and **What** (the tangible products or services offered). Most brands communicate from the outside in — they start with What and never reach Why. Great brands start from the inside out.

The method matters because customers do not buy what you do; they buy why you do it. A brand with a clear Why attracts people who share the same belief, creating loyalty that survives product pivots, pricing changes, and competitive pressure. For early-stage founders, the Why also serves as a hiring filter, a decision-making tenet, and an emotional anchor when the roadmap shifts. The Why is not a tagline. It is a statement of belief that predates your product and will outlast any single feature.

This framework synthesises prior M3 brand work — particularly the Brand Prism culture facet, Brand Archetypes motivation, and JTBD emotional jobs — into a purpose-driven narrative. The output feeds directly into Brand Positioning (Why becomes the core of differentiation), Messaging Architecture (Why becomes the brand promise layer), Tone of Voice (Why sets the emotional register), M4 Design Brief (Why gives visual identity its direction), and M5 Market Validation (Why provides messaging hypotheses to test).

## Task 1: Extract Purpose Signals From Prior Work

**Goal:** Gather and categorise raw material about founder motivation, brand culture, and customer emotion from all completed upstream frameworks.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Brand Prism document (especially culture facet) | `brand_prism.md` applied framework | Yes |
| JTBD analysis (emotional and social jobs) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/jtbd.md` | Yes |
| Project memo (Tenets section) | `projects/[project]/founder/[project]_memo.md` | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (leader quote, "why it matters" narrative) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/working_backwards.md` | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes analysis (primary archetype motivation) | `brand_archetypes.md` applied framework | No |
| Founder's personal motivation notes or diary entries | M1/M2/M3 founder diaries, conversations | No |

**Action:**

1. Re-read the **Brand Prism culture facet**. Extract every phrase that describes what the brand believes, values, or stands for. These are the strongest Why signals because culture is the facet closest to purpose.
2. Re-read the **JTBD emotional jobs**. For each emotional job, ask: "What must we believe about the world for this emotional outcome to matter?" Write the implied belief alongside each job.
3. Re-read the **project memo Tenets**. Tenets encode the founder's operating beliefs. Highlight any tenet that is about purpose or values rather than execution tactics.
4. Re-read the **Working Backwards leader quote and "why it matters" section**. Extract the founder's own words about why this product should exist in the world.
5. If available, review the **Brand Archetypes primary motivation** (e.g., the Hero's motivation is mastery and proving worth; the Creator's motivation is realising vision). Note how the archetype's universal motivation maps to this specific brand.
6. If available, review **founder diary entries** for personal stories about why the founder started this project. Look for recurring themes: frustration, aspiration, injustice, wonder, necessity.
7. Compile all extracted signals into a **Purpose Signals Brief** organised into three categories:
   - **Founder beliefs** (what the founder personally cares about beyond revenue).
   - **Customer emotional needs** (what customers need to feel or become).
   - **Cultural values** (what the brand stands for in the market).

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Purpose Signals Brief | Categorised list (founder beliefs, customer emotional needs, cultural values) | Raw material for drafting the Why statement in Task 2 |

**Validation:**

- [ ] You have at least **three distinct purpose signals** from at least two different source frameworks.
- [ ] At least one signal comes from the **founder's own words** (diary, leader quote, or direct conversation).
- [ ] At least one signal connects to a **customer emotional job** — purpose is not purely inward-looking.
- [ ] No signal is a product feature, revenue target, or market-sizing claim in disguise.

## Task 2: Define The Why

**Goal:** Articulate the brand's purpose, cause, or belief in a single sentence that goes beyond profit, connects to the customer's emotional job, and is authentic to the founder.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Purpose Signals Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| Brand Prism culture facet | Applied framework | Yes |
| JTBD emotional jobs | Applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Review the Purpose Signals Brief. Identify the **one recurring theme** that appears across founder beliefs, customer emotional needs, and cultural values. This convergence point is the strongest Why candidate.
2. Draft 3-5 candidate Why statements using this structure: "We believe that **[statement of belief about the world]**." Do not mention your product, category, or features.
3. Test each candidate against three filters:
   - **Endurance:** Would this Why still be true if you pivoted to a completely different product in the same domain?
   - **Authenticity:** Would the founder say this at a dinner table without feeling like they were performing?
   - **Customer resonance:** Does this belief connect to an emotional job your customers actually experience?
4. Eliminate candidates that fail any filter. If multiple candidates pass, choose the one that is most **specific** to this brand — not a generic truth that any company could claim (e.g., "We believe in making the world better" is too generic).
5. Write the selected Why as a **single sentence**. Then write a **supporting paragraph** (3-5 sentences) that explains the belief in concrete terms, references the Brand Prism culture facet, and connects to at least one JTBD emotional job.
6. Read the Why aloud. If it sounds like a mission statement on a corporate website, rewrite it in plainer language.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Why statement | Single sentence expressing the brand's core belief | Anchors all downstream brand work in purpose |
| Why narrative | Supporting paragraph (3-5 sentences) | Provides context, traceability to Brand Prism and JTBD |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The Why statement does **not mention** your product name, category, or any feature.
- [ ] The Why would survive a **complete product pivot** — it describes a belief, not a solution.
- [ ] You can draw a direct line from the Why to at least one **JTBD emotional job**.
- [ ] The founder can read the Why aloud and say "Yes, I genuinely believe this" without hesitation.
- [ ] The Why is **specific enough** that a competitor in an adjacent space would not use the same words.

---

## Task 3: Define The How

**Goal:** Describe the unique approach, values, or process through which the brand delivers on its Why differently from alternatives.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Why statement and narrative | Task 2 | Yes |
| Brand Prism personality and relationship facets | Applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas Unfair Advantage block | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/lean_canvas.md` | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes analysis (behavioural traits) | Applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Re-read the Why statement. Ask: "If we believe this, how does that belief change the way we build, sell, and support our product compared to alternatives?"
2. From the **Brand Prism personality facet**, extract traits that describe how the brand acts (e.g., "transparent", "rigorous", "playful"). These traits shape the How.
3. From the **Brand Prism relationship facet**, extract the type of relationship the brand has with customers (e.g., "trusted advisor", "creative partner", "empowering coach"). This relationship style is part of the How.
4. From the **Lean Canvas Unfair Advantage**, extract any structural, process, or capability differentiators that are hard to copy.
5. Draft **2-3 How principles** — short statements that describe the brand's differentiated approach. Use this structure: "We **[action/approach]** because we believe **[connection to Why]**."
6. For each How principle, verify that it is:
   - **Observable** — a customer or team member could see it in action.
   - **Differentiating** — competitors do not operate this way (or do not prioritise it).
   - **Connected to Why** — it flows logically from the stated belief.
7. Order the How principles by importance. The first principle should be the most distinctive and visible.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| How principles | 2-3 numbered statements describing the brand's differentiated approach | Bridges the abstract Why to the concrete What |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Each How principle connects **explicitly** to the Why statement — remove the Why and the How loses its rationale.
- [ ] A customer could **observe** each How principle in the product experience, communication, or support.
- [ ] At least one How principle reflects the **Lean Canvas Unfair Advantage** — How is not purely aspirational.
- [ ] The How principles are **distinct from competitors'** stated values or approaches.

---

## Task 4: Define The What

**Goal:** State the tangible products or services the brand offers, connecting them explicitly back to Why and How.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Why statement and How principles | Tasks 2-3 | Yes |
| Lean Canvas Solution block | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/lean_canvas.md` | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR headline and solution paragraph | Applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Re-read the **Lean Canvas Solution block** and the **Working Backwards PR headline**. These describe What you offer in customer-facing language.
2. Draft a **What statement** (1-2 sentences) that names the product category and primary offering. Use customer language, not internal terminology.
3. Verify the What connects to How and Why by writing the full chain in one paragraph: "We believe **[Why]**. That is why we **[How]**. And that is how we deliver **[What]**."
4. Read the chain aloud. If any link feels forced or disconnected, revisit the weaker element. The chain must flow naturally from belief to approach to offering.
5. Confirm that the What does **not** try to do the work of Why or How. Common mistake: embedding purpose language into the product description ("our revolutionary platform that empowers..."). The What is deliberately the simplest layer — a plain description of the offering.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| What statement | 1-2 sentences describing the tangible product or service | Completes the Golden Circle with the concrete offering layer |
| Why-How-What chain | Single paragraph connecting all three layers | Demonstrates internal coherence of the Golden Circle |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The What statement names a **recognisable product category** that the target customer already understands.
- [ ] The What is **plain and specific** — no purpose language, no superlatives, no jargon.
- [ ] The Why-How-What chain reads as a **coherent narrative** from belief to offering.
- [ ] Removing the Why and How would make the What indistinguishable from a generic competitor description — the layers above are what give it meaning.

## Task 5: Validate Authenticity And Wire Into Brand Artefacts

**Goal:** Test that the complete Golden Circle is enduring, authentic, and motivating. Then document it and connect to downstream brand frameworks.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Complete Golden Circle (Why, How, What) | Tasks 2-4 | Yes |
| Brand Prism document | Applied framework | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes analysis | Applied framework | Yes |
| Founder input (verbal or written) | Direct conversation or diary | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Run the **Endurance Test**: Imagine the brand pivots to a different product in the same domain (e.g., from SaaS analytics to consulting services). Does the Why still hold? Does the How still apply? If not, the Why is too product-specific — revise it.
2. Run the **Authenticity Test**: Present the Why to the founder without context. Ask: "Does this feel true to you? Would you say this to a friend?" If the founder hesitates or qualifies, the Why needs revision. The founder must own the Why viscerally, not intellectually.
3. Run the **Motivation Test**: Present the Why to a potential team member or early customer. Ask: "Does this make you want to be part of this?" If it generates indifference, the Why lacks emotional charge — it may be accurate but uninspiring.
4. Run the **Consistency Check**: Verify that the Golden Circle is consistent with:
   - Brand Prism culture facet (Why should echo culture).
   - Brand Prism personality facet (How should echo personality).
   - Brand Archetypes primary motivation (Why should resonate with archetype motivation).
5. Document discrepancies. If the Golden Circle contradicts an earlier framework output, decide which is more accurate and update the weaker artefact. Record the change in M3 founder diary.
6. Save the **Golden Circle document** to `projects/[project]/founder/brand/golden_circle.md` with:
   - Why statement and narrative.
   - How principles (2-3).
   - What statement.
   - Why-How-What chain paragraph.
   - Validation results (which tests passed, any revisions made).
7. Wire the Golden Circle into downstream frameworks:
   - **Brand Positioning**: Use the Why as the foundation for the positioning statement's benefit and differentiation claims.
   - **Messaging Architecture**: Use the Why as the brand promise layer; How principles as supporting message pillars.
   - **Tone of Voice**: Use the Why's emotional register to calibrate tone dimensions (e.g., a Why rooted in empowerment suggests a confident, direct tone).
   - **M4 Design Brief**: Include the Why as the visual identity's emotional direction (e.g., a Why rooted in clarity suggests clean, minimal design).
   - **M5 Market Validation**: Include the Why statement as a messaging hypothesis to test with target customers.
8. Update M3 founder diary with the Golden Circle decision: Why chosen, alternatives considered, validation results.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Golden Circle document | Applied framework in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/golden_circle.md` | Canonical purpose reference for all downstream brand and product work |
| Downstream wiring notes | Annotations in Positioning, Messaging, Tone of Voice briefs | Ensures the Why propagates consistently through the brand system |
| M3 founder diary entry | Row(s) in `m3_founder_diary.md` | Captures the purpose decision, alternatives, and validation results |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The Why survived the **Endurance Test** — it holds across product pivots.
- [ ] The Why survived the **Authenticity Test** — the founder owns it without hesitation.
- [ ] The Why survived the **Motivation Test** — it inspires beyond the founding team.
- [ ] The Golden Circle is **consistent** with Brand Prism culture, personality, and archetype motivation.
- [ ] The Golden Circle document exists at project level and is referenced by Positioning, Messaging, and Tone of Voice frameworks.

## Pitfalls

**Confusing Why With What You Do**

The most common error is stating the Why as a description of your product or service. "We exist to build the best analytics platform" is a What masquerading as a Why. The Why must describe a belief about the world that exists independently of your current product.
Instead: Strip all product, feature, and category language from the Why. Ask: "Would this statement still be meaningful if we stopped making this product tomorrow?"

**Writing A Generic Purpose Statement**

Statements like "We believe in empowering people" or "We exist to make the world a better place" are true for every company and therefore meaningless. A good Why is specific enough that some people would disagree with it.
Instead: Test specificity by asking whether a direct competitor could use the same Why without changing a word. If yes, your Why is too generic. Add the specific perspective, context, or value that makes it yours.

**Skipping The How Layer**

Some teams jump from a lofty Why directly to product features. The How layer is the bridge that makes the Why operational. Without it, the Why becomes a poster on the wall that has no influence on decisions.
Instead: Write 2-3 How principles and verify that each one changes at least one concrete decision (hiring, feature prioritisation, customer communication, pricing).

**Treating Golden Circle As A Marketing Exercise**

The Golden Circle is a strategy tool, not a copywriting exercise. If the Why only appears on the website's About page and nowhere else, it has failed. The Why should influence product decisions, hiring criteria, partnership choices, and what the team says no to.
Instead: After completing the Golden Circle, identify three specific decisions the team has made (or will make) differently because of the Why.

**Defining Purpose Without Founder Input**

An AI agent, brand consultant, or team member cannot define the Why on behalf of the founder. The Why must come from the founder's genuine beliefs and motivations. External parties can extract, articulate, and challenge — but not invent.
Instead: Always surface the candidate Why to the founder for gut-check validation. If the founder cannot say "I believe this" with conviction, the Why is wrong regardless of how elegant it sounds.

## Success Criteria

- [ ] A **Why statement** exists as a single sentence expressing the brand's core belief, and it does not mention the product or any features.
- [ ] The Why connects to at least one **JTBD emotional job** and is traceable to the **Brand Prism culture facet**.
- [ ] **2-3 How principles** exist that are observable, differentiating, and connected to the Why.
- [ ] A **What statement** exists that names the product category in plain customer language.
- [ ] The **Why-How-What chain** reads as a coherent narrative from belief to offering.
- [ ] The Golden Circle passed the **Endurance, Authenticity, and Motivation tests**.
- [ ] A Golden Circle applied framework document exists in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/golden_circle.md`.
- [ ] Brand Positioning, Messaging Architecture, and Tone of Voice frameworks reference the Golden Circle outputs.
- [ ] M3 founder diary contains at least one entry documenting the purpose decision and validation results.

## For AI Agents

**Execution Context:**

1. Read `brand_process.md` and confirm that Brand Prism (Step 3) has been completed before starting Golden Circle.
2. Read the user's Brand Prism document, JTBD analysis (emotional jobs), project memo Tenets, and Working Backwards PR/FAQ before beginning Task 1.
3. Track progress: "Currently in Task [N]: [Task Name]".
4. Confirm each task's validation checklist before proceeding to the next task.
5. NEVER invent a Why statement on the founder's behalf. Extract, propose, and challenge — but always surface the candidate Why for founder gut-check before finalising.
6. Record key Golden Circle decisions and validation results in M3 founder diary as they emerge.

**Working With Tasks:**

- Complete each task atomically. Do not skip the Endurance, Authenticity, or Motivation tests in Task 5.
- If the founder is unavailable for the Authenticity Test, flag this as a blocker and propose a written alternative (e.g., present 2-3 candidate Why statements and ask the founder to choose and annotate).
- When the Golden Circle contradicts an earlier Brand Prism or Archetypes output, surface the discrepancy and propose which artefact to update. Do not silently override.
- Treat the Golden Circle as a living artefact; suggest revisiting when later milestones reveal shifts in founder conviction, customer emotional jobs, or market positioning.
