# Working Backwards (PR/FAQ Framework)

**Purpose:** Task-based guide for using Amazon's Working Backwards PR/FAQ methodology to turn a vague idea into a sharp, testable product vision in M1 Conception.

**Context:**  
Use in M1 Conception, primarily in step "Define Customer and Problem" and before locking Lean Canvas or JTBD artifacts.

---

## Framework Overview

Start from a **future press release and FAQs** that describe a product at launch, in the language of real customers.

**The PR/FAQ:**
- A **one-page press release** answering:
  1. Who is the customer?
  2. What problem are you solving?
  3. What is the solution (in customer language)?
  4. Why will customers adopt it?
- A **split FAQ**:
  - **External FAQ:** Questions that customers, partners, and external stakeholders would ask.
  - **Internal FAQ:** Hard questions about feasibility, economics, dependencies, and strategy—culminating in: **Is it worth doing?**

The goal is to **kill or sharpen ideas** before they consume real resources.

---

## Task Structure

| Task | Goal | Key Inputs | Key Outputs |
|------|------|------------|-------------|
| 1. Clarify Customer And Problem | Turn a vague idea into a precise customer/problem definition | Raw idea, early research, founder intuition | Customer & Problem Brief |
| 2. Draft The Press Release | Write a one-page launch PR that answers the four core customer questions | Customer & Problem Brief, founder vision | Press Release v1 |
| 3. Draft The External FAQ | Anticipate and answer realistic customer and partner questions | Press Release v1, Customer & Problem Brief | External FAQ v1 |
| 4. Draft The Internal FAQ | Surface feasibility, economics, and strategic questions and answers | Press Release v1, External FAQ v1, basic business/tech inputs | Internal FAQ v1 (incl. "Is it worth doing?") |
| 5. Review, Debate, And Iterate | Run a narrative-style review, refine or kill the idea based on evidence | PR/FAQ v1, key stakeholders, any available data | Approved PR/FAQ or kill/park decision + revision backlog |
| 6. Connect To Downstream Frameworks | Translate PR/FAQ outputs into JTBD, Lean Canvas, and founder log | Approved (or latest) PR/FAQ, milestone process doc | Updated M1 artifacts and logged decisions/assumptions |

## Task 1: Clarify Customer And Problem

**Goal:** Produce a concise, evidence-informed description of the primary customer, their context, and the specific problem you intend to solve.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Raw idea description (1–3 paragraphs or a short pitch) | Founder/team | Yes |
| Any existing customer conversations or notes | Founder log, CRM, research notes | No |
| High-level market context (who else plays in this space) | Desk research, domain knowledge | No |

**Action:**

1. Write a **one-paragraph idea synopsis**: who you think you serve, what you imagine building, and why now.
2. Enumerate **candidate customer segments** (job title, company size, industry, geography, usage context). Aim for 3–7 distinct segment hypotheses.
3. For each segment, list:
   - The primary **job-to-be-done** in their own words.
   - The current **workarounds or alternatives**.
   - The most painful **frictions or failure modes**.
4. Choose **one primary segment** for the first PR/FAQ. Use criteria:
   - Pain intensity and frequency.
   - Willingness and ability to pay.
   - Ease of reaching them in M5 Market Validation.
5. Draft a **Customer & Problem Brief** (max 1 page) that includes:
   - A one-sentence description of the primary customer.
   - A one-paragraph description of their context.
   - A clearly worded problem statement that does **not** mention your solution.
   - 3–5 concrete examples of the problem "in the wild".
6. Circulate the brief with at least one other person (co-founder, advisor, domain expert) and refine language until they can restate the customer and problem in their own words.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Customer & Problem Brief | 0.5–1 page narrative or structured doc | Locks in **who** you serve and **what** hurts before you touch features |

**Validation:**

- [ ] You can name **at least three real organisations or people** who fit the primary customer description.
- [ ] Your problem statement can be read **without mentioning your product** and still makes sense.
- [ ] A neutral third party can paraphrase the customer and problem after one read of the brief.
- [ ] You can list at least **two current alternatives** customers use today (including "do nothing").

## Task 2: Draft The Press Release

**Goal:** Write a one-page press release, set at a future launch date, that answers the four core customer questions in clear, compelling language.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Customer & Problem Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| High-level product vision and constraints | Founder/team | Yes |
| Any existing product mockups or sketches | Design tools, whiteboards | No |

**Action:**

1. Choose a **realistic future launch date** (typically 6–24 months out) and a plausible launch context (event, blog, email).
2. Draft the press release with the following structure (1 page max):
   1. **Headline** – One line capturing the core customer benefit (not the technology).
   2. **Subheading** – Names the primary customer and what changes for them.
   3. **Dateline** – Location and future launch date.
   4. **Intro Paragraph** – Context for the announcement; what category you are in.
   5. **Problem Paragraph** – Vivid description of the customer's pain and current alternatives.
   6. **Solution Paragraph** – How your product solves that pain in customer language.
   7. **Leader Quote** – A quote from a founder or product leader explaining why this matters.
   8. **How It Works** – One or two paragraphs walking through a typical customer journey.
   9. **Customer Quote** – Hypothetical quote from a named archetypal customer.
   10. **Getting Started** – How to access, pricing model at a high level, onboarding friction.
3. **Remove internal jargon and implementation detail**. If a non-technical reader would stumble, rewrite.
4. Read the press release aloud. Highlight any sentence that:
   - Does not clearly tie back to the customer and problem.
   - Sounds like aspirational fluff rather than a concrete claim.
   - Depends on unvalidated assumptions you cannot yet justify.
5. Revise until the PR can be read standalone by a stranger and still convey:
   - Who it is for.
   - What problem it solves.
   - Why it is different/better.
   - How to get started.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Press Release v1 | One-page narrative (text only) | Primary artefact that encodes the customer-centric vision and answers questions 1–4 |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The press release fits on **one page** at a readable font size.
- [ ] The primary customer and problem are clear in the first two paragraphs.
- [ ] At least one person from outside the core team finds it compelling and can restate the value proposition.
- [ ] There is **no internal jargon** or implementation detail that a typical customer would not care about.

## Task 3: Draft The External FAQ

**Goal:** Enumerate and answer realistic questions that customers, partners, and external stakeholders will ask when they encounter your press release.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Press Release v1 | Task 2 | Yes |
| Customer & Problem Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| Any existing customer interviews or surveys | Research, founder log | No |

**Action:**

1. Brainstorm a long list (20–40) of potential **external questions**, grouped into themes:
   - Product understanding ("What is it?", "How does it work?").
   - Eligibility and fit ("Is this for companies like mine?", "What if we are small/large?").
   - Pricing and commercial terms.
   - Integration and migration.
   - Data, privacy, and trust.
   - Support and reliability.
2. Prioritise down to **10–20 core questions** that:
   - A serious buyer is likely to ask before adopting.
   - Reveal hidden complexity or trade-offs in your current idea.
3. For each question, draft an answer that:
   - Uses **customer language**, not internal jargon.
   - Is specific enough to be meaningful (avoid "it depends" without framing ranges or examples).
   - Acknowledges limitations and trade-offs honestly.
4. Check coverage against the press release:
   - Every bold claim in the PR must have at least one FAQ that explains constraints, pricing, or edge cases.
   - Every major risk a savvy buyer would worry about must appear as a question.
5. Flag questions where you **cannot yet answer credibly**:
   - Mark them as **Assumption – requires validation**.
   - Add them to a separate list for Leap of Faith or Validation frameworks.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| External FAQ v1 | Q&A document grouped by theme | Surfaces customer-facing questions and forces clarity on promises, trade-offs, and adoption barriers |

**Validation:**

- [ ] You have at least **10 substantial external questions** with concrete answers.
- [ ] For each major promise in the PR, there is at least one FAQ that clarifies limits, pricing, or conditions.
- [ ] At least one person representing (or close to) the target customer confirms that these are indeed the **right questions**.
- [ ] All "we don't know yet" answers are explicitly tagged as assumptions to validate.

## Task 4: Draft The Internal FAQ

**Goal:** Make explicit the feasibility, economic, and strategic questions that determine whether the idea is worth doing—and answer them as far as current knowledge allows.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Press Release v1 | Task 2 | Yes |
| External FAQ v1 | Task 3 | Yes |
| Rough business assumptions (TAM, pricing, costs) | Founder/team, early modelling | Yes (even if rough) |
| Initial technical perspective | Tech lead/architect, if available | No but strongly recommended |

**Action:**

1. Create internal FAQ themes:
   - **Feasibility and resources** (team, technology, time).
   - **Economics** (TAM/SAM/SOM, unit economics, pricing logic).
   - **Dependencies and risks** (partners, regulation, data, platform risk).
   - **Strategic fit** (alignment with vision, cannibalisation, differentiation).
   - **Success metrics and kill criteria**.
2. Within each theme, pose frank internal questions:
   - "What capabilities must we build that we do not have today?"
   - "What are the top three technical unknowns?"
   - "What has to be true about customer behaviour for our unit economics to work?"
   - "What would make us kill this project after 3–6 months?"
3. Answer each question as concretely as possible:
   - Use ranges and scenarios instead of fake precision (for example, "We estimate TAM between €200–400M based on X and Y assumptions").
   - Clearly mark **assumptions** vs **known facts**.
   - Link to any supporting artefacts (early models, architecture sketches).
4. Explicitly answer the fifth core question: **"Is it worth doing?"**
   - Summarise the economic potential (magnitude, time to impact).
   - Summarise the main risks and unknowns.
   - Propose an initial **investment thesis** (for example, "Worth a 3‑month validation sprint if we can de-risk A and B.").
5. Distil the internal FAQ into:
   - A short **Executive Summary** (half page) capturing the "worth doing?" argument.
   - A list of **top 5 assumptions** that must be validated next.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Internal FAQ v1 | Structured Q&A plus executive summary | Forces explicit treatment of feasibility, economics, strategy, and kill criteria |

**Validation:**

- [ ] There is a written, defensible answer to **"Is it worth doing?"**—even if the answer is "Not yet" or "Only if we validate X/Y".
- [ ] Top risks and assumptions are clearly listed and traceable to specific questions.
- [ ] A technical stakeholder (if available) has reviewed and either signed off or annotated risks.
- [ ] There is **no major hidden dependency** that is only in someone's head.

## Task 5: Review, Debate, And Iterate

**Goal:** Use the PR/FAQ as a decision tool in a structured review meeting to either sharpen, park, or kill the idea.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Press Release v1 | Task 2 | Yes |
| External FAQ v1 | Task 3 | Yes |
| Internal FAQ v1 | Task 4 | Yes |
| List of key stakeholders | Founder, leadership, tech, go-to-market | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Schedule a **PR/FAQ review session** (60–90 minutes) with:
   - Founder(s) and core builders.
   - At least one person who will challenge assumptions (playing "Bar Raiser").
2. Before the meeting:
   - Circulate the PR/FAQ at least 24 hours in advance.
   - Ask participants to add **inline comments and questions** directly in the document.
3. In the meeting:
   - Start with **silent reading** of the PR/FAQ (10–20 minutes), even if participants have seen it before.
   - Collect top questions and disagreements before diving into detailed edits.
4. Structure the discussion around:
   - Are we convinced about the **customer and problem**?
   - Are the **promises in the PR** realistically achievable?
   - Do the **internal FAQs** surface the real risks and economics?
   - What would have to be true to justify a serious investment?
5. At the end of the session, make an explicit call:
   - **Advance:** Proceed to structured validation (M2) with a clear list of assumptions to test.
   - **Revise And Re-run:** Substantial issues found; owners and deadlines for a v2 PR/FAQ.
   - **Park:** Idea has potential but is not priority now; document why and when to revisit.
   - **Kill:** Idea is not worth pursuing; document the reasons and link them in the founder log.
6. Capture all decisions, dissenting views, and assigned follow-ups in the **milestone founder log**.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| PR/FAQ Review Notes + Decision | Meeting notes, updated document comments | Turns PR/FAQ into a real decision artefact, not a static document |

**Validation:**

- [ ] There is a **clear written decision**: advance, revise, park, or kill.
- [ ] Follow-up actions (for example, "run JTBD interviews on segments A and B") are assigned owners and deadlines.
- [ ] Founder log contains a short entry capturing the decision and rationale.

## Task 6: Connect To Downstream Frameworks

**Goal:** Wire the PR/FAQ outputs into other M1 frameworks and prepare for M2 Validation.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Latest approved (or current) PR/FAQ | Tasks 2–5 | Yes |
| M1 Conception process doc | `conception_process.md` | Yes |
| Templates for JTBD, Lean Canvas, Problem-Solution Fit, Leap of Faith | Framework templates in `m1_conception` and `m2_validation` | No but recommended |

**Action:**

1. From the **Customer & Problem Brief** and PR:
   - Populate the **primary JTBD statements** for the chosen segment.
   - Extract concrete **pains, gains, and existing alternatives** to feed into Problem-Solution Fit work.
2. From the **Internal FAQ**:
   - Extract key assumptions (e.g., adoption rates, pricing, sales cycle) into a **Leap of Faith / assumption map**.
   - Identify the **few metrics** that will define success in early validation (conversion, retention, willingness to pay).
3. From the PR/FAQ as a whole:
   - Fill the **Lean Canvas** blocks (Problem, Customer Segments, Unique Value Proposition, Solution, Channels, Revenue/Cost, Key Metrics, Unfair Advantage) using PR/FAQ language.
4. Update the **founder log** with:
   - A link to the PR/FAQ.
   - Key decisions and assumptions.
   - The chosen next frameworks (e.g., "Next: JTBD + Lean Canvas + initial Leap of Faith").
5. If relevant, brief the **Prototypation (M4)** owners:
   - Share the PR/FAQ as the **north star** for early landing pages, flows, and visual artefacts.
   - Highlight which claims must be visually and interactively expressed in prototypes.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Updated JTBD, Lean Canvas, Problem-Solution/Leap of Faith notes, founder log entries | Set of documents and log entries | Ensures PR/FAQ drives concrete modelling, research, and later prototyping/validation |

**Validation:**

- [ ] JTBD, Lean Canvas, and other selected frameworks explicitly reference the PR/FAQ.
- [ ] Top 3–5 assumptions from the Internal FAQ are present in the validation backlog.
- [ ] Founder log documents how Working Backwards shaped the chosen direction.

## Pitfalls

**Writing For Stakeholders, Not Customers**

Ruthlessly strip internal language and write as if your only audience is a sceptical but busy target customer.

**Treating PR/FAQ As Marketing Copy**

Treat the PR/FAQ as a **pre-mortem plus commitment device**; write the awkward questions into the Internal FAQ and mark unknowns explicitly.

**Skipping The Internal FAQ**

Never consider a PR "done" until the Internal FAQ answers "Is it worth doing?" in writing.

**Over-Specifying Implementation**

Keep implementation detail in separate technical docs; use the PR/FAQ to lock **what must be true for the customer**, not how you will implement it.

**Never Killing Ideas**

Define in advance what evidence would make you kill or park the idea, and record kill decisions proudly as **successes of the method**.

**Workflow Position:**

## Success Criteria

- [ ] A **Customer & Problem Brief** exists and names real, identifiable customers.
- [ ] A one-page **Press Release** answers the four core customer questions without internal jargon.
- [ ] **External** and **Internal** FAQs exist, with assumptions and risks explicitly tagged.
- [ ] There is a written, shared answer to **"Is it worth doing?"** (advance, revise, park, or kill).
- [ ] Key assumptions and next steps are wired into JTBD, Lean Canvas, and validation frameworks.
- [ ] The founder log contains at least one clear entry linking to the PR/FAQ and capturing decisions.

## For AI Agents

**Execution Context:**

1. Read `conception_process.md` before starting.
2. Identify the user's current milestone and artefacts.
3. Track progress: "Currently in Task [N]: [Task Name]".
4. Never skip the Internal FAQ or the explicit "Is it worth doing?" conclusion.
5. Record key decisions and assumptions in M1 founder log.

**Working With Tasks:**

- Complete each task atomically.
- When the user cannot answer an Internal FAQ question, label it as an assumption and suggest follow-up frameworks.
- When a kill or park decision emerges, document it clearly and link it to underlying assumptions.
