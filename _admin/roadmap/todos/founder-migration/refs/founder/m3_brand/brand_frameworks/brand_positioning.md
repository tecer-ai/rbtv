# Brand Positioning Statement

**Purpose:** Task-based guide for crafting a brand positioning statement and perceptual map that define the brand's unique market position, in M3 Brand Step 5.

**Context:**
Read `brand_process.md` before starting. Execute in M3 Brand Step 5: Craft Positioning Statement. MUST complete Golden Circle first. Uses all prior brand frameworks plus M2 TAM/SAM/SOM competitive context.

---

## Framework Overview

Brand positioning defines the specific place a brand occupies in the customer's mind relative to alternatives. The positioning statement follows a precise template: "For [target segment] who [need/job], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit], unlike [primary alternative] which [key limitation]." Every word in this sentence is a strategic choice. The target locks your audience. The need grounds you in a real job. The category sets expectations. The benefit is your promise. The alternative and limitation define the competitive frame.

This framework also produces a **Perceptual Map** — a two-dimensional visualisation that plots competitors and your brand along two axes chosen for strategic relevance (e.g., simple vs. powerful, self-serve vs. full-service, affordable vs. premium). The map makes competitive positioning visible and reveals white space — positions that no competitor currently occupies. White space is not automatically valuable; it must coincide with a real customer need. The map forces you to defend why your chosen position is both differentiated and desirable.

The methodology draws from Al Ries and Jack Trout's foundational work on mental positioning and April Dunford's modern approach to category design. Both traditions agree: positioning is not what you do to a product, it is what you do to the mind of the prospect. A positioning statement is not a tagline — it is an internal strategic document that aligns every downstream decision: messaging, visual identity, pricing, feature prioritisation, and go-to-market. This framework synthesises the Golden Circle (Why and How become the benefit and differentiation mechanism), Brand Prism (physique, personality, and relationship shape the competitive frame), Lean Canvas (UVP, Segments, Unfair Advantage provide strategic inputs), and M2 TAM/SAM/SOM (competitive landscape provides the alternatives and limitations).

## Task 1: Consolidate Positioning Inputs

**Goal:** Gather and organise every strategic input needed for positioning from all completed upstream frameworks into a single brief.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Golden Circle document (Why, How, What) | `golden_circle.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Brand Prism document (physique, personality, relationship facets) | `brand_prism.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas (UVP, Customer Segments, Unfair Advantage) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/lean_canvas.md` | Yes |
| M2 TAM/SAM/SOM and competitive landscape | `projects/[project]/founder/validation/` | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (key differentiation claims) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/working_backwards.md` | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes analysis (character and behavioural traits) | `brand_archetypes.md` applied framework | No |
| JTBD analysis (primary job, functional and emotional dimensions) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/jtbd.md` | No |

**Action:**

1. From the **Golden Circle**, extract:
   - The **Why** — this is the emotional foundation of your benefit claim.
   - The **How principles** — these are the differentiation mechanisms.
   - The **What** — this defines the category frame.
2. From the **Brand Prism**, extract:
   - **Physique** — tangible characteristics that customers associate with the brand.
   - **Personality** — character traits that shape how the brand shows up in the market.
   - **Relationship** — the type of bond the brand creates with customers (e.g., trusted advisor, empowering coach).
3. From the **Lean Canvas**, extract:
   - **UVP** — the current value proposition hypothesis.
   - **Customer Segments** (including Early Adopters) — who you position for.
   - **Unfair Advantage** — what makes your position defensible.
4. From **M2 TAM/SAM/SOM**, extract:
   - The **competitive landscape** — who the alternatives are, what they claim, how they position.
   - Market sizing context — which segments are large enough to matter.
5. From **Working Backwards**, extract:
   - **Key differentiation claims** from the PR headline, solution paragraph, and leader quote.
   - Customer language that resonates (from the customer quote and FAQ).
6. Compile everything into a **Positioning Inputs Brief** structured as:
   - **Target**: Who we position for (segment, role, situation).
   - **Need**: What job or problem we address (JTBD primary job, Lean Canvas Problem).
   - **Benefit**: What we promise (Golden Circle Why + How).
   - **Category**: What kind of thing we are (Golden Circle What, market category).
   - **Alternatives**: Who we position against (M2 competitive landscape, Lean Canvas alternatives).
   - **Differentiators**: Why we are different (How principles, Unfair Advantage, Brand Prism personality).

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Positioning Inputs Brief | Structured document with six sections (Target, Need, Benefit, Category, Alternatives, Differentiators) | Organises all upstream strategic material for drafting the positioning statement |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Every section of the Positioning Inputs Brief is populated with **specific, traceable content** from upstream frameworks — not fresh brainstorming.
- [ ] You can name at least **three specific competitors or alternatives** (including "do nothing") with their positioning claims.
- [ ] The Target section describes a **specific segment and situation**, not a demographic label.
- [ ] The Benefit section connects to the Golden Circle **Why**, not just to product features.

## Task 2: Draft The Positioning Statement

**Goal:** Write a single-sentence positioning statement using the template structure, with each element deliberately chosen and annotated.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Positioning Inputs Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| Golden Circle Why and How | Applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas UVP | Applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Use the positioning statement template:
   - "For **[target segment]** who **[need/job]**, **[brand]** is the **[category]** that **[key benefit]**, unlike **[primary alternative]** which **[key limitation]**."
2. Fill each element using the Positioning Inputs Brief:
   - **[target segment]**: Use the Target section. Be specific enough that the reader can picture a real person or organisation. Avoid broad labels like "businesses" or "professionals".
   - **[need/job]**: Use the Need section. State the functional or emotional job in customer language. This should echo the JTBD primary job.
   - **[brand]**: Your brand name.
   - **[category]**: Use the Category section. Choose a category the target already recognises. If you are creating a new category, use a modifier on an existing one (e.g., "collaborative analytics platform" rather than inventing an unrecognisable term).
   - **[key benefit]**: Use the Benefit section. State the single most important outcome the customer gets. This should connect to the Golden Circle Why — the benefit is purpose made tangible.
   - **[primary alternative]**: Use the Alternatives section. Choose the single alternative your target is most likely using today. This is often not a direct competitor — it may be a spreadsheet, a manual process, or a different category of tool.
   - **[key limitation]**: State the specific shortcoming of that alternative that your benefit addresses. This must be a real, acknowledged limitation, not a straw man.
3. Draft 2-3 variations of the positioning statement. Vary the target specificity, category frame, and competitive alternative.
4. For each variation, annotate the element choices:
   - Why did you choose this target over a broader or narrower one?
   - Why this category and not an adjacent one?
   - Why this alternative and not another?
5. Select the strongest variation using these criteria:
   - **Uniqueness**: No competitor could use this exact statement.
   - **Defensibility**: You can deliver on the benefit claim with current or near-term capabilities.
   - **Relevance**: The target cares deeply about the stated benefit.
   - **Credibility**: The claim is believable given what the target knows about you.
6. Write the final draft as a clean, single sentence. Remove any hedging language ("helps", "aims to", "strives to"). Use direct, assertive language.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Draft positioning statement | Single sentence following the template | Defines the brand's unique market position |
| Element annotations | Short notes explaining each element choice | Makes strategic reasoning explicit and reviewable |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The positioning statement is a **single sentence** that follows the template structure completely.
- [ ] Each element is **specific** — a reader unfamiliar with your brand can picture the target, category, and competitive frame.
- [ ] The benefit connects to the Golden Circle **Why** — it is not a feature claim but a purpose-driven outcome.
- [ ] The alternative and limitation are **real and acknowledged** by the target market, not a straw man.

## Task 3: Create A Perceptual Map

**Goal:** Build a two-dimensional visualisation of competitive positioning that reveals white space and validates the brand's intended position.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Positioning Inputs Brief (Alternatives and Differentiators) | Task 1 | Yes |
| M2 TAM/SAM/SOM competitive landscape | Applied framework | Yes |
| Draft positioning statement | Task 2 | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Choose **two dimensions** for the perceptual map axes. Each dimension must be:
   - **Meaningful to the target segment** — customers actually evaluate alternatives along this axis.
   - **Differentiating** — competitors spread out along this axis rather than clustering.
   - **Strategic** — your brand's position on this axis connects to the Golden Circle How or Lean Canvas Unfair Advantage.
2. Common dimension pairs for digital products (choose or adapt):
   - Simple vs. Powerful (ease of use vs. depth of capability).
   - Self-serve vs. Full-service (autonomy vs. hand-holding).
   - Affordable vs. Premium (price positioning).
   - Generalist vs. Specialist (breadth vs. depth of domain focus).
   - Automated vs. Manual (degree of human involvement).
   - Individual vs. Team (single-user vs. collaborative).
3. List **5-8 competitors and alternatives** from the M2 competitive landscape. Include:
   - Direct competitors (same category, same target).
   - Indirect competitors (different category, same job-to-be-done).
   - The "do nothing" or manual workaround (spreadsheets, email, etc.).
4. Plot each competitor on the two-dimensional map. Use best available evidence (product reviews, pricing pages, feature comparisons, customer interviews). Do not guess — if you lack data on a competitor's position, note the uncertainty.
5. Plot your brand's **intended position** on the map. This is where you want to be, not necessarily where you are today.
6. Identify **white space** — areas of the map where no competitor is positioned. For each white space:
   - Ask: "Is there a real customer need in this space, or is it empty because nobody wants to be there?"
   - If the white space coincides with a real need, it is a strategic opportunity.
   - If the white space exists because customers do not value that combination, it is a trap.
7. Document the perceptual map as a simple text-based diagram or table with:
   - Axis labels and definitions.
   - Competitor positions with brief justifications.
   - Your brand's intended position with a one-sentence rationale.
   - White space analysis.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Perceptual map | Two-dimensional plot (text diagram or table) with competitor positions and brand position | Visualises competitive positioning and reveals white space |
| White space analysis | Short narrative (2-3 paragraphs) | Explains whether identified white space represents real opportunity or a dead zone |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Both axes represent dimensions that the **target segment actually uses** to evaluate alternatives — not dimensions that only matter internally.
- [ ] At least **5 competitors or alternatives** are plotted, including at least one indirect competitor and the "do nothing" option.
- [ ] Your brand's intended position is **distinct** from every plotted competitor — no overlapping positions.
- [ ] White space is analysed for **desirability**, not just emptiness — empty positions are not automatically valuable.

## Task 4: Test Differentiation And Credibility

**Goal:** Verify that the positioning is unique among plotted competitors, credible given current capabilities, and relevant to the target's primary job.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Draft positioning statement | Task 2 | Yes |
| Perceptual map | Task 3 | Yes |
| Current product capabilities and roadmap | Founder/team knowledge | Yes |
| JTBD primary job | Applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Run the **Uniqueness Test**:
   - Read the positioning statement and check it against each competitor plotted on the perceptual map.
   - For each competitor, ask: "Could this competitor credibly make the same claim?" If yes, the positioning is not unique — revise the benefit, alternative, or category frame.
   - A strong position can only be claimed by one brand. If two brands could say the same thing, the position is undifferentiated.
2. Run the **Credibility Test**:
   - List the **evidence** that supports the key benefit claim: existing product capabilities, customer testimonials, technical architecture, team expertise, partnerships.
   - Ask: "If a sceptical target customer investigated this claim, what would they find?"
   - If the claim is aspirational (not yet delivered), explicitly mark it as a **forward-looking position** and identify the gap between current state and claimed position. Document a plan to close the gap within a specific timeframe.
3. Run the **Relevance Test**:
   - Compare the positioning statement's benefit to the **JTBD primary job**. The benefit must directly address the functional or emotional progress the customer is trying to make.
   - Ask: "If the target customer read this statement, would they immediately recognise their situation and need?" If not, the positioning is accurate but irrelevant — the benefit does not match the job.
4. Run the **Consistency Test**:
   - Verify the positioning statement is consistent with:
     - Golden Circle Why (benefit connects to purpose).
     - Brand Prism physique and personality (category and tone match brand identity).
     - Lean Canvas UVP (positioning amplifies rather than contradicts the value proposition).
   - Document any inconsistencies and resolve them by updating the weaker artefact.
5. If any test fails, return to Task 2 and revise the positioning statement. Iterate until all four tests pass.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Validated positioning statement | Single sentence (revised if needed) with test results | Confirmed market position that is unique, credible, relevant, and consistent |
| Test results documentation | Short notes per test (pass/fail, evidence, revisions) | Makes the validation reasoning explicit and auditable |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The positioning statement passed the **Uniqueness Test** — no plotted competitor can credibly make the same claim.
- [ ] The positioning statement passed the **Credibility Test** — evidence exists or a gap-closing plan is documented.
- [ ] The positioning statement passed the **Relevance Test** — the benefit directly addresses the JTBD primary job.
- [ ] The positioning statement passed the **Consistency Test** — it aligns with Golden Circle, Brand Prism, and Lean Canvas.

## Task 5: Document And Wire Into Messaging Architecture

**Goal:** Save the validated positioning statement and perceptual map, then connect them to downstream brand frameworks.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Validated positioning statement | Task 4 | Yes |
| Perceptual map and white space analysis | Task 3 | Yes |
| Downstream framework briefs (Messaging Architecture, Tone of Voice, M4 Design Brief) | `brand_process.md` | Yes |
| M3 founder diary | `projects/[project]/founder/brand/m3_founder_diary.md` | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Save the **Brand Positioning document** to `projects/[project]/founder/brand/brand_positioning.md` with the following sections:
   - Positioning statement (single sentence).
   - Element annotations (explaining each strategic choice).
   - Perceptual map (two-dimensional plot with competitor positions).
   - White space analysis.
   - Test results (Uniqueness, Credibility, Relevance, Consistency).
   - Positioning inputs traceability (links to Golden Circle, Brand Prism, Lean Canvas, M2).
2. Wire the positioning into **Messaging Architecture** (M3 Step 6):
   - The **brand promise layer** in the messaging hierarchy derives directly from the positioning statement's benefit claim.
   - The **competitive differentiation** in messaging derives from the alternative and limitation.
   - The **audience framing** uses the target segment and need from the positioning statement.
3. Wire the positioning into **Tone of Voice** (M3 Step 7):
   - The positioning statement's category and benefit imply a **register** (e.g., a premium position demands a more authoritative tone; a simplicity position demands a more conversational tone).
   - The perceptual map position on any relevant axis (e.g., playful vs. serious) should align with tone dimensions.
4. Wire the positioning into **M4 Design Brief**:
   - The positioning statement's category, benefit, and competitive frame inform **visual positioning** — how the brand should look relative to competitors.
   - The perceptual map's axes can translate directly to visual dimensions (e.g., simple vs. powerful maps to minimal vs. feature-rich UI patterns).
5. Wire the positioning into **M5 Market Validation**:
   - The positioning statement becomes a **messaging hypothesis** to test with target customers.
   - The perceptual map axes become **evaluation criteria** for competitive testing.
6. Update M3 founder diary with:
   - The final positioning statement.
   - Key trade-offs made (which target, category, alternative, and benefit were chosen over alternatives).
   - Perceptual map insights (white space identified, competitive threats noted).

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Brand Positioning document | Applied framework in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/brand_positioning.md` | Canonical positioning reference for all downstream brand and go-to-market work |
| Downstream wiring notes | Annotations in Messaging Architecture, Tone of Voice, Design Brief, Market Validation briefs | Ensures positioning propagates consistently through the brand system |
| M3 founder diary entry | Row(s) in `m3_founder_diary.md` | Captures the positioning decision, trade-offs, and competitive insights |

**Validation:**

- [ ] A Brand Positioning document exists at project level with the statement, map, white space analysis, and test results.
- [ ] The **Messaging Architecture** brief references the positioning statement's benefit as the brand promise.
- [ ] The **Tone of Voice** brief references the positioning to calibrate register.
- [ ] The **M4 Design Brief** references the perceptual map for visual positioning.
- [ ] The M3 founder diary documents the positioning decision and key trade-offs.

## Pitfalls

**Positioning On Features Instead Of Outcomes**

Feature-based positioning ("the only platform with real-time sync and 50+ integrations") is fragile because competitors copy features. Outcome-based positioning ("the only platform that gives you a single source of truth before your Monday standup") is durable because it anchors to a job.
Instead: Write the positioning benefit as a customer outcome, not a product capability. Test by asking: "Is this benefit still meaningful if the underlying feature changes?"

**Choosing A Category Nobody Recognises**

Inventing a new category ("predictive collaboration intelligence") forces you to educate the market before you can sell. This is expensive and slow for early-stage brands. Use a recognisable category with a modifier.
Instead: Start with a category your target already searches for or budgets for (e.g., "project management tool"). Add a modifier that captures your differentiation (e.g., "AI-first project management for remote engineering teams"). Earn the right to a new category only after achieving market validation.

**Positioning Against A Straw Man**

Choosing a weak alternative and attributing an obvious limitation makes the positioning feel dishonest. If the "unlike" clause describes a limitation everyone already knows about, the positioning adds no information.
Instead: Choose the strongest relevant alternative — the one your target is most likely using today — and identify a limitation that is real, specific, and hard for that alternative to fix given their architecture or business model.

**Ignoring The Perceptual Map**

Some teams write the positioning statement and skip the map. Without the map, you cannot see whether your claimed position is actually distinct or whether you are sitting on top of a competitor. The map is not optional decoration — it is the visual proof of differentiation.
Instead: Build the perceptual map before finalising the positioning statement. Use the map to verify that your intended position occupies a distinct space that coincides with a real customer need.

**Confusing Positioning With Messaging**

The positioning statement is a strategic internal document. It should never appear verbatim on a website, ad, or pitch deck. Messaging translates positioning into audience-appropriate language. If you publish the positioning statement as-is, it will read as jargon.
Instead: Treat the positioning statement as the source code and messaging as the compiled output. The Messaging Architecture framework (M3 Step 6) handles the translation. Keep the positioning statement internal and use it to judge whether messaging is on-strategy.

1. Read `brand_process.md` and confirm that Golden Circle (Step 4) has been completed before starting Brand Positioning.
2. Read the user's Golden Circle document, Brand Prism document, Lean Canvas, M2 TAM/SAM/SOM, and Working Backwards PR/FAQ before beginning Task 1.
3. Track progress: "Currently in Task [N]: [Task Name]".

**Working With Tasks:**

- Complete each task atomically. Do not skip the perceptual map — it is required, not optional.
- If M2 TAM/SAM/SOM competitive data is incomplete, flag this as a gap and propose lightweight competitive research (e.g., review competitor websites, pricing pages, G2/Capterra reviews) before proceeding.
- When the positioning statement fails a test in Task 4, return to Task 2 and revise. Do not accept a positioning statement that fails any test.
- When the positioning contradicts an earlier Golden Circle or Brand Prism output, surface the discrepancy and propose which artefact to update. Do not silently override.
- Treat the positioning as a living artefact; suggest revisiting when M5 Market Validation reveals that the target, benefit, or competitive frame has shifted.
