# Messaging Architecture

**Purpose:** Task-based guide for building a hierarchical messaging structure that ensures consistent, audience-specific communication across all touchpoints in M3 Brand Step 6.

**Context:**
Read `brand_process.md` before starting. Execute in M3 Brand Step 6: Build Messaging Architecture. MUST complete Brand Positioning Statement (Step 5) first. Uses the positioning statement as the foundation for all messaging layers.

---

## Framework Overview

Messaging Architecture is a hierarchical communication structure that moves from a single brand promise at the top through key messages, proof points, and calls to action at the bottom. The methodology originates from content strategy and B2B marketing practice, formalized by practitioners like Margot Bloomstein and adapted for startup brand-building. The core principle: every piece of communication a brand produces must trace upward to a single, unifying promise and downward to concrete evidence that makes the promise credible.

Most startups skip this step entirely and write ad hoc copy for each touchpoint. The result is fragmented messaging where the landing page says one thing, the investor deck says another, and the onboarding flow says a third. Messaging Architecture prevents this by defining the hierarchy once and then deriving all copy from it. Each audience (early adopters, mainstream customers, partners, investors) receives messages tailored to their concerns, but every message connects to the same brand promise and the same proof points.

This framework matters because it bridges the gap between strategic brand identity (archetypes, prism, positioning) and tactical execution (prototype copy, sales scripts, marketing campaigns). Without it, the brand frameworks from Steps 2-5 remain abstract concepts that never reach the customer. With it, every word the brand produces is intentional, traceable, and testable.

## Task 1: Define Brand Promise Layer

**Goal:** Distill the Brand Positioning Statement into a single, customer-facing brand promise that is emotionally resonant and connects to the Golden Circle Why.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Brand Positioning Statement | `brand_positioning.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Golden Circle document (Why, How, What) | `golden_circle.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Brand Prism (Culture, Relationship facets) | `brand_prism.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR headline and customer quote | `working_backwards.md` applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Re-read the Brand Positioning Statement. Identify the core benefit claim (the "that [key benefit]" clause). This is the rational foundation of your promise.
2. Re-read the Golden Circle Why. Identify the emotional register: what belief or cause does this brand champion? This is the emotional foundation of your promise.
3. Combine rational benefit and emotional register into a single sentence that a customer would understand without any context about your company. Use the structure: "[Emotional aspiration] through [rational mechanism]" or "[What changes for you] because [why we exist]."
4. Test the draft promise against three filters:
   - **Customer-facing:** Would a real customer nod and say "yes, that is what I want"? If it sounds like an internal strategy statement, rewrite.
   - **Emotionally resonant:** Does it connect to a feeling (confidence, relief, pride, control), not just a feature? If it reads like a spec sheet, rewrite.
   - **Differentiated:** Could a competitor claim the exact same promise without changing a word? If yes, sharpen it using your positioning's competitive frame.
5. Compare the draft promise to the Working Backwards PR headline and customer quote. The promise should feel like the distilled essence of both. If it contradicts either, reconcile.
6. Write the final brand promise as one sentence, maximum 15 words. Document the rationale showing how it connects to positioning (rational) and Golden Circle Why (emotional).

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Brand promise statement | Single sentence (max 15 words) with documented rationale | Top of messaging hierarchy; all other messages must support this promise |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The brand promise is one sentence that a customer can understand without industry jargon.
- [ ] The promise connects explicitly to the Golden Circle Why (emotional) and Positioning Statement (rational).
- [ ] A competitor cannot claim the identical promise without modification.
- [ ] The promise does not mention product features, technology, or internal capabilities.
- [ ] Reading the Working Backwards PR headline and this promise side by side, they reinforce each other.

## Task 2: Create Key Messages (3-5)

**Goal:** For each major audience, define 3-5 key messages that support the brand promise. Each message must trace to a validated assumption or framework output.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Brand promise statement | Task 1 | Yes |
| JTBD analysis (job stories, forces, customer language) | `jtbd.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Brand Prism (all six facets) | `brand_prism.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas (Customer Segments, UVP, Problem) | `lean_canvas.md` applied framework | Yes |
| M2 Validation findings (validated assumptions) | `projects/[project]/founder/validation/` | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes (themes, narrative patterns) | `brand_archetypes.md` applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Define your primary audiences explicitly. For most startups, these are:
   - **Early adopters:** The first users who feel the pain most acutely and tolerate rough edges.
   - **Mainstream customers:** The larger segment you will grow into after early traction.
   - **Partners:** Technology, distribution, or integration partners who extend your reach.
   - **Investors:** Seed, Series A, or strategic investors who fund growth.
2. For each audience, identify what they care about most by pulling from upstream frameworks:
   - Early adopters: JTBD primary job, push forces (pain with status quo), emotional job.
   - Mainstream customers: JTBD related jobs, pull forces (what attracts them to a new solution), anxieties.
   - Partners: Lean Canvas Channels, Unfair Advantage, integration opportunities.
   - Investors: Lean Canvas Problem, Customer Segments, Key Metrics, market size from M2 TAM/SAM/SOM.
3. For each audience, draft 3-5 key messages that:
   - Support the brand promise (every message must connect upward).
   - Address what that specific audience cares about.
   - Use language that audience actually uses (pull from JTBD interviews, not internal vocabulary).
4. For each key message, document its **traceability**: which framework output or validated assumption supports it. Use a simple annotation: "[Source: JTBD primary job]" or "[Source: M2 validated assumption #3]."
5. Test each message against Brand Prism consistency: does this message align with the brand's Culture, Personality, and Relationship facets? If a message contradicts the Prism, revise the message (not the Prism).
6. Eliminate messages that:
   - Are features in disguise ("We have real-time analytics" is a feature, not a message).
   - Cannot be traced to any upstream framework or validated data.
   - Overlap substantially with another message for the same audience.
7. Finalize 3-5 key messages per audience, each with a one-sentence summary and a traceability annotation.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Key messages per audience | Structured list: audience, message, traceability annotation | Middle layer of hierarchy; bridges promise to proof |
| Audience-message matrix | Table mapping audiences to their key messages | Quick reference for anyone writing audience-specific copy |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Each audience has 3-5 distinct key messages that do not overlap significantly.
- [ ] Every key message explicitly supports the brand promise (you can draw a line from message to promise).
- [ ] Every key message has a documented traceability annotation to a framework output or validated assumption.
- [ ] No key message is a feature statement; all describe outcomes, beliefs, or emotional shifts.
- [ ] Key messages for early adopters use language drawn from JTBD customer interviews, not internal jargon.

## Task 3: Build Proof Points Per Message

**Goal:** For each key message, define 2-3 proof points that make the message credible. Proof points are evidence: data, customer quotes, features framed as benefits, or third-party validation.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Key messages per audience | Task 2 | Yes |
| JTBD analysis (customer quotes, workarounds, forces) | `jtbd.md` applied framework | Yes |
| M2 Validation data (experiment results, survey data, interview findings) | `projects/[project]/founder/validation/` | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (claims, features, customer quote) | `working_backwards.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas Solution block (top features/capabilities) | `lean_canvas.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Problem-Solution Fit (before/after emotions, constraints) | `problem_solution_fit.md` applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. For each key message, ask: "Why should the audience believe this?" List every piece of evidence you currently have:
   - **Data points:** M2 validation results, survey percentages, experiment outcomes, market sizing numbers.
   - **Customer language:** Direct quotes from JTBD interviews that echo the message. Use verbatim customer words, not paraphrased versions.
   - **Feature-as-benefit:** A product capability framed as what it enables for the customer. Always state the benefit, not the feature ("reduces reporting time from 4 hours to 20 minutes" not "has automated reports").
   - **Third-party validation:** Industry analyst citations, press coverage, advisor endorsements, pilot results.
2. Select the 2-3 strongest proof points per message. Strongest means: most specific, most verifiable, most emotionally compelling to the target audience.
3. For each proof point, document:
   - The proof point itself (one sentence).
   - The source (which framework, interview, or data set it comes from).
   - The type (data, customer quote, feature-benefit, third-party).
4. Flag any key message that has fewer than 2 credible proof points. This message is aspirational, not proven. Either:
   - Demote it to a "future message" that requires more validation, or
   - Design an M5 experiment specifically to generate the missing proof.
5. Cross-check proof points against Brand Prism Culture and Relationship facets. Proof points must not contradict the brand identity (for example, citing aggressive competitive benchmarks if the brand personality is collaborative).
6. Organize proof points into a Proof Point Library: a flat table with columns for Message, Proof Point, Source, Type, and Status (validated/hypothetical).

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Proof Point Library | Table: Message, Proof Point, Source, Type, Status | Evidence base for all brand communications; reusable across touchpoints |
| Flagged gaps | List of messages lacking sufficient proof | Feeds into M5 validation experiment design |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Every key message has at least 2 proof points with documented sources.
- [ ] At least one proof point per message uses customer language from JTBD interviews.
- [ ] At least one proof point per message references M2 validation data or a concrete metric.
- [ ] No proof point contradicts the Brand Prism or Positioning Statement.
- [ ] Messages with fewer than 2 validated proof points are flagged and have a plan for generating evidence.

## Task 4: Define Calls To Action Per Journey Stage

**Goal:** Map specific CTAs to customer journey stages and distribution channels, ensuring the right ask appears at the right moment.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Key messages and proof points | Tasks 2-3 | Yes |
| Lean Canvas Channels block | `lean_canvas.md` applied framework | Yes |
| JTBD job map (stages of progress) | `jtbd.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Customer journey stages (Awareness, Consideration, Decision, Retention) | Standard framework | Yes |
| Problem-Solution Fit (constraints, adoption barriers) | `problem_solution_fit.md` applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Define four journey stages and the customer's mindset at each:
   - **Awareness:** Customer recognizes the problem but has not evaluated solutions. Mindset: curious, skeptical, time-constrained.
   - **Consideration:** Customer is actively comparing options. Mindset: analytical, risk-aware, looking for proof.
   - **Decision:** Customer is ready to commit. Mindset: needs final reassurance, wants to minimize switching cost.
   - **Retention:** Customer is using the product and deciding whether to expand or refer. Mindset: evaluating ROI, looking for deeper value.
2. For each journey stage, define the primary CTA:
   - Awareness: "Learn more" actions (read a blog post, watch a demo video, download a guide, attend a webinar).
   - Consideration: "Try or compare" actions (start a free trial, request a demo, view case studies, compare pricing).
   - Decision: "Buy or sign up" actions (create an account, start a paid plan, schedule onboarding, sign a contract).
   - Retention: "Expand or refer" actions (upgrade plan, invite team members, leave a review, refer a colleague).
3. For each CTA, specify:
   - The **channel** where it appears (informed by Lean Canvas Channels: website, email, social, partner integration, sales conversation).
   - The **key message** it connects to (which message from Task 2 sets up this CTA).
   - The **proof point** that supports it (which evidence removes the barrier at this stage).
4. Cross-reference CTAs against the JTBD job map. Ensure that CTAs at each stage align with where the customer is in their job progression (Define, Locate, Prepare, Execute, Confirm, Evolve).
5. Check for journey gaps: is there a stage where you have no CTA or no supporting message? If so, create the missing element or flag it for M4/M5 development.
6. Build a CTA Matrix: a table with columns for Journey Stage, CTA, Channel, Supporting Message, Supporting Proof Point.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| CTA Matrix | Table: Journey Stage, CTA, Channel, Supporting Message, Supporting Proof Point | Maps the right ask to the right moment across all channels |
| Journey gap analysis | List of stages or channels lacking CTAs | Feeds into M4 prototype planning and M5 experiment design |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Every journey stage (Awareness, Consideration, Decision, Retention) has at least one defined CTA.
- [ ] Every CTA connects to a specific key message and proof point (no orphan CTAs).
- [ ] CTAs use channels identified in the Lean Canvas Channels block.
- [ ] The progression from Awareness to Retention CTAs represents increasing commitment (learn, try, buy, expand).
- [ ] No CTA asks for more commitment than the journey stage warrants (do not ask for a purchase at the Awareness stage).

## Task 5: Document Architecture And Wire Into Tone Of Voice

**Goal:** Save the complete hierarchical structure, create audience-specific message cards, and connect the architecture to downstream frameworks for execution.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Brand promise | Task 1 | Yes |
| Key messages per audience | Task 2 | Yes |
| Proof Point Library | Task 3 | Yes |
| CTA Matrix | Task 4 | Yes |
| Brand Prism (all facets) | `brand_prism.md` applied framework | Yes |
| Brand Archetypes (primary archetype) | `brand_archetypes.md` applied framework | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Assemble the complete Messaging Architecture document with this hierarchy:
   - **Level 1 — Brand Promise:** Single sentence at the top.
   - **Level 2 — Key Messages:** 3-5 messages per audience, organized by audience.
   - **Level 3 — Proof Points:** 2-3 proof points per message, with source and type.
   - **Level 4 — Calls to Action:** CTAs by journey stage, linked to messages and proof points.
2. Create **Audience Message Cards** — one-page summaries for each audience that include:
   - Audience name and description (who they are, what they care about).
   - Their 3-5 key messages.
   - Top proof points for each message.
   - Primary CTAs and channels.
   - These cards are the quick-reference tools that anyone writing copy will use.
3. Document explicit wiring to downstream frameworks:
   - **Tone of Voice (Step 7):** The key messages and proof points become the raw material for tone application. Tone of Voice will define how these messages sound; Messaging Architecture defines what they say.
   - **M4 Prototypation:** Prototype copy (landing page headlines, onboarding text, CTA buttons) must pull directly from this architecture. Reference specific messages and CTAs by ID.
   - **M5 Market Validation:** A/B test messaging variants, SPIN selling scripts, and smoke test copy must use messages from this architecture. This ensures validation tests are testing brand-consistent messaging, not random copywriting.
   - **M6 MVP:** Production marketing copy, onboarding flows, and support templates inherit from this architecture.
4. Save the document as the applied framework at `projects/[project]/founder/brand/messaging_architecture.md`.
5. Update the M3 founder diary with key messaging decisions: which audiences were prioritized, which messages were hardest to prove, and which gaps need M5 validation.
6. Update the project memo Progress > Brand section to note that Messaging Architecture is complete and list the primary brand promise.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Messaging Architecture document | Applied framework in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/` | Canonical reference for all brand messaging across audiences and touchpoints |
| Audience Message Cards | One-page summaries per audience within the document | Quick-reference for copywriting across M4, M5, M6 |
| Downstream wiring notes | Section in the document | Ensures M4, M5, M6 teams know where to pull messaging from |
| Updated founder diary and project memo | Existing project artifacts | Captures key messaging decisions and progress |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The Messaging Architecture document contains all four hierarchy levels: promise, messages, proof points, CTAs.
- [ ] Each audience has a complete message card with messages, proof points, and CTAs.
- [ ] Every element in the hierarchy traces upward (CTAs to messages, messages to promise) and has documented traceability.
- [ ] The document explicitly references Tone of Voice, M4, M5, and M6 as downstream consumers.
- [ ] The M3 founder diary records at least one entry about messaging decisions made during this framework.

## Pitfalls

**Writing Slogans Instead Of Strategy**

A brand promise is not a tagline for an ad campaign. Slogans are creative executions; the brand promise is a strategic commitment that every message must support. If your promise sounds like something a copywriter would put on a billboard, it is too superficial. Instead: write a promise that could serve as the first sentence of a strategy document. It should be clear and directional, not clever and ambiguous.

**Treating All Audiences The Same**

Early adopters, mainstream customers, partners, and investors have fundamentally different concerns, risk tolerances, and vocabularies. A single set of messages for all audiences produces communications that are vaguely relevant to everyone and deeply compelling to no one. Instead: build distinct message sets per audience, each tailored to what that audience actually cares about, while keeping all messages anchored to the same brand promise.

**Proof Points Without Sources**

Unattributed proof points are opinions, not evidence. Claiming "customers love our product" without a specific quote, metric, or data point is worse than saying nothing — it trains the audience to distrust your claims. Instead: document the source for every proof point. If a proof point has no source, flag it as hypothetical and design an M5 experiment to validate it before using it in public-facing copy.

**Feature-Stuffing The Message Layer**

Key messages describe outcomes, beliefs, and emotional shifts — not product capabilities. "We offer real-time collaboration" is a feature. "Your team ships decisions in hours, not weeks" is a message. When messages read like a feature list, the architecture collapses into a spec sheet that no audience connects with emotionally. Instead: use the JTBD "When / I want to / so I can" structure as a gut-check. If a message does not map to a job outcome, rewrite it.

**Skipping The Journey Stage Mapping**

Defining messages without CTAs and journey context produces a beautiful document that nobody knows how to use. Messages need activation: who sees them, when, and what do we ask them to do next? Instead: always complete Task 4. The CTA Matrix is what transforms the architecture from a strategy artifact into an operational tool.

**Ignoring M2 Validation Data**

Building messaging on assumptions rather than validated data produces messages that sound good internally but fail with real customers. If M2 has validated (or invalidated) specific claims, the messaging architecture must reflect that evidence. Instead: treat M2 validation findings as mandatory inputs. Flag every message whose supporting evidence is hypothetical versus validated.

## Success Criteria

This framework is complete when:

- [ ] A brand promise exists as a single sentence (max 15 words) with documented rationale connecting to Golden Circle Why and Positioning Statement.
- [ ] 3-5 key messages exist for each defined audience (early adopters, mainstream customers, partners, investors), each with traceability annotations.
- [ ] A Proof Point Library exists with 2-3 proof points per key message, each attributed to a specific source and typed (data, customer quote, feature-benefit, third-party).
- [ ] A CTA Matrix exists mapping CTAs to journey stages (Awareness, Consideration, Decision, Retention) with channel, message, and proof point links.
- [ ] Audience Message Cards exist as one-page quick-reference summaries per audience.
- [ ] The Messaging Architecture document explicitly references Tone of Voice, M4, M5, and M6 as downstream consumers.
- [ ] Every key message traces upward to the brand promise and downward to at least 2 proof points.
- [ ] Messages with insufficient proof are flagged and have a plan for M5 validation.
- [ ] The M3 founder diary contains at least one entry documenting messaging decisions.
- [ ] The project memo Progress > Brand section references the completed Messaging Architecture.
