# Brand Archetypes

**Purpose:** Task-based guide for using Carl Jung's 12 universal archetypes to define brand character and personality in M3 Brand Step 2.

**Context:**
Read `brand_process.md` before starting. Execute in M3 Brand Step 2: Identify Brand Archetype. This is the first brand framework. MUST complete M1 Conception (JTBD, Working Backwards, Lean Canvas) and M2 Validation before starting.

---

## Framework Overview

Every brand occupies a character role in the customer's mind whether the founder chooses it deliberately or not. Carl Jung identified twelve universal archetypes that recur across cultures, myths, and stories because they map to fundamental human motivations. Carol S. Pearson and Margaret Mark adapted this model for brand strategy, demonstrating that the most resonant brands consistently embody one dominant archetype. When a brand's archetype aligns with its customer's deepest emotional and social jobs, the brand stops competing on features and starts competing on meaning.

The twelve archetypes and their core motivations are: **The Innocent** (safety and optimism), **The Explorer** (freedom and discovery), **The Sage** (understanding and wisdom), **The Hero** (mastery and achievement), **The Outlaw** (liberation and revolution), **The Magician** (transformation and power), **The Regular Guy** (belonging and authenticity), **The Lover** (intimacy and passion), **The Jester** (enjoyment and play), **The Caregiver** (service and protection), **The Creator** (innovation and self-expression), **The Ruler** (control and stability).

Selecting an archetype is not a creative exercise detached from business reality. The archetype must be grounded in what your JTBD analysis revealed about how customers want to feel (emotional jobs) and how they want to be perceived (social jobs). A brand that chooses the Hero archetype while its customers crave safety and belonging will feel inauthentic. This framework forces you to derive archetype selection from evidence, not aspiration, and to express that archetype in concrete brand behaviours that feed directly into Brand Prism, Tone of Voice, and downstream M4 design work.

## Task 1: Extract Emotional Territory From M1/M2

**Goal:** Consolidate all emotional and social evidence from prior milestones into a single brief that grounds archetype selection in customer reality, not founder preference.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| JTBD analysis (emotional jobs, social jobs, forces) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/jtbd.md` | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (customer narrative, leader quote, customer quote) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/working_backwards.md` | Yes |
| M2 Validation findings (validated assumptions about customer pain and motivation) | `projects/[project]/founder/validation/` | Yes |
| Lean Canvas (UVP, Customer Segments) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/lean_canvas.md` | No |
| Problem-Solution Fit Canvas (emotions before/after, triggers) | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/problem_solution_fit.md` | No |

**Action:**

1. From JTBD, extract every **emotional job** (how customers want to feel) and **social job** (how they want to be perceived). List them verbatim.
2. From Working Backwards, extract the **customer quote** and any language describing how the customer's life changes. Highlight emotional words and aspirational phrases.
3. From M2 Validation, extract any **validated assumptions** about what motivates customers, what they fear, and what they aspire to. Discard assumptions that were refuted.
4. From Problem-Solution Fit (if available), extract the **emotions before** (frustration, anxiety, helplessness) and **emotions after** (confidence, relief, pride) columns.
5. Cluster the extracted material into three categories:
   - **Core motivations**: What drives them to act (fear of failure, desire for mastery, need for belonging, pursuit of freedom).
   - **Emotional pain points**: How they feel when the problem is active (overwhelmed, embarrassed, stuck, anxious).
   - **Social stakes**: How they want others to perceive them (competent, innovative, reliable, visionary).
6. Draft an **Emotional Territory Brief** (half page maximum) that synthesises these clusters into a concise portrait of the customer's emotional landscape.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Emotional Territory Brief | Half-page narrative with clustered motivations, pains, and social stakes | Grounds archetype evaluation in validated customer evidence rather than founder intuition |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Every motivation, pain, or social stake in the brief can be traced to a **specific JTBD job, customer quote, or validated assumption**.
- [ ] The brief includes at least **two emotional jobs** and at least **one social job**.
- [ ] A neutral reader can understand the customer's emotional world without seeing the source artefacts.
- [ ] No motivation listed is based solely on founder preference or aspiration; each has customer evidence.

## Task 2: Evaluate All Twelve Archetypes

**Goal:** Systematically score each of the twelve archetypes against your customer's emotional territory, your brand's purpose, and the competitive landscape.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Emotional Territory Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| Lean Canvas UVP and Unfair Advantage | `projects/[project]/founder/conception/lean_canvas.md` | Yes |
| Competitor brand audit (informal or formal) | Desk research, competitor websites, social presence | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (leader quote, tone, purpose language) | Applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Create an **Archetype Evaluation Matrix** with twelve rows (one per archetype) and five columns:
   - **Archetype** (name and core motivation).
   - **Emotional Fit** (0-3): How well does this archetype's motivation match the customer's emotional and social jobs?
   - **Purpose Fit** (0-3): How naturally does this archetype express your brand's purpose and UVP?
   - **Differentiation** (0-3): How distinct is this archetype from competitors' brand positioning?
   - **Total** (sum of three scores, max 9).
2. For **Emotional Fit**, compare each archetype's core motivation against the Emotional Territory Brief:
   - Score 3 if the archetype directly embodies the customer's primary emotional or social job.
   - Score 2 if it relates strongly but is not the primary match.
   - Score 1 if it is tangentially relevant.
   - Score 0 if it contradicts or is irrelevant to the customer's emotional territory.
3. For **Purpose Fit**, ask: "If our brand were a character in the customer's story, would this archetype naturally deliver our UVP and fulfil our stated purpose?"
   - Score 3 for a natural, authentic fit.
   - Score 0 for a forced or contradictory fit.
4. For **Differentiation**, audit competitors:
   - List the top 3-5 competitors and identify which archetype each most closely embodies (even if they have not done this exercise formally).
   - Score 3 if no competitor occupies this archetype in your category.
   - Score 2 if one competitor partially occupies it.
   - Score 1 if it is crowded.
   - Score 0 if a dominant competitor already owns this archetype.
5. Calculate totals. Highlight the top 3-4 scoring archetypes for deeper consideration in Task 3.
6. Write a short rationale (2-3 sentences) for each of the top-scoring archetypes explaining why it scored well.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Archetype Evaluation Matrix | Table with 12 rows, scores, and totals | Provides systematic, evidence-based evaluation of all archetype options |
| Competitor archetype map | Short list of top competitors and their approximate archetypes | Ensures differentiation is considered alongside emotional and purpose fit |
| Top archetype rationales | 2-3 sentences per top scorer | Prepares the selection decision in Task 3 |

**Validation:**

- [ ] All twelve archetypes have been scored; none were skipped or dismissed without evaluation.
- [ ] Emotional Fit scores are **traceable** to specific items in the Emotional Territory Brief.
- [ ] At least **3 competitors** have been mapped to approximate archetypes.
- [ ] The top-scoring archetypes have written rationales, not just numbers.

## Task 3: Select Primary And Secondary Archetypes

**Goal:** Make a decisive, justified archetype selection: one primary archetype that will dominate brand character, and optionally one secondary archetype that adds nuance.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Archetype Evaluation Matrix with top scorers | Task 2 | Yes |
| Emotional Territory Brief | Task 1 | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (overall tone and promise) | Applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Review the top 3-4 archetypes from the evaluation matrix. For each, ask:
   - Can we **authentically sustain** this character across every customer touchpoint (website, support, onboarding, error messages, pricing page)?
   - Does this archetype **feel true** to the founding team's values, or would maintaining it require constant pretending?
   - Would this archetype be **immediately recognisable** to our target customer as "a brand that gets me"?
2. Select **one primary archetype**. Write a one-paragraph justification that references:
   - The specific emotional and social jobs it serves.
   - The purpose and UVP alignment.
   - The competitive differentiation it provides.
3. Optionally select **one secondary archetype** if:
   - The primary archetype leaves a significant emotional job unaddressed.
   - The secondary archetype adds nuance without contradicting the primary.
   - The combination creates a distinctive brand character (for example, Sage + Explorer = a brand that teaches through discovery, not lectures).
4. If selecting a secondary, define the **blend ratio**: the primary should dominate (roughly 70-80% of brand expression), while the secondary provides texture (20-30%).
5. Explicitly document archetypes you **rejected** and why, so future team members understand the reasoning.
6. Write a one-sentence **Archetype Statement**: "[Brand] is primarily the [Archetype] because [reason], with elements of the [Secondary Archetype] that [what it adds]."

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Primary archetype selection with justification | One-paragraph narrative | Locks the dominant brand character with evidence-based reasoning |
| Secondary archetype selection (optional) with blend ratio | Short narrative | Adds nuance without diluting the primary character |
| Rejected archetypes with reasons | Bulleted list | Prevents future revisiting without new evidence |
| Archetype Statement | One sentence | Provides a memorable anchor for all downstream brand work |

**Validation:**

- [ ] The primary archetype scored in the **top two** of the evaluation matrix, or there is an explicit written reason for overriding the scores.
- [ ] The justification references **at least one emotional job** and **at least one differentiation factor**.
- [ ] If a secondary archetype is chosen, it does not **contradict** the primary (for example, Outlaw + Ruler is almost always incoherent).
- [ ] At least **three rejected archetypes** have documented reasons for rejection.
- [ ] The founding team can read the Archetype Statement aloud and say "yes, that is us" without hesitation.

## Task 4: Define Archetype Expression

**Goal:** Translate the selected archetype into concrete, actionable guidance for how the brand manifests in voice, visual style, customer relationship, and content themes.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Selected primary and secondary archetypes | Task 3 | Yes |
| JTBD emotional/social jobs | Applied framework | Yes |
| Working Backwards PR/FAQ (tone, customer language) | Applied framework | Yes |
| Lean Canvas (Customer Segments, Channels) | Applied framework | No |

**Action:**

1. Define **Voice Character** (how the brand sounds):
   - List 3-5 adjectives that describe the archetype's voice (for example, the Sage sounds "clear, calm, authoritative, curious, precise").
   - List 3-5 adjectives the voice is **not** (for example, the Sage does not sound "brash, casual, sentimental, vague, flashy").
   - Write 2-3 sample sentences in the brand's voice addressing the customer's primary emotional job.
2. Define **Visual Style Direction** (how the brand looks):
   - Describe the visual mood the archetype evokes (for example, the Explorer evokes "open spaces, natural textures, movement, warm earth tones").
   - List visual elements to **embrace** and elements to **avoid**.
   - Note: This is directional guidance for M4 Design Brief, not a finished visual identity.
3. Define **Customer Relationship Type** (how the brand relates to the customer):
   - Name the relationship dynamic (for example, Sage = teacher-student, Hero = coach-athlete, Caregiver = protector-protected, Explorer = fellow adventurer).
   - Describe what the brand promises in this relationship and what it expects from the customer.
   - Describe how this relationship manifests in onboarding, support, and crisis moments.
4. Define **Content Themes** (what the brand talks about):
   - List 4-6 recurring themes or topics the archetype naturally gravitates toward.
   - List 2-3 themes the archetype **avoids** or handles carefully.
   - Connect each theme to a specific customer emotional or social job.
5. If a secondary archetype is selected, note where it modifies the primary expression:
   - Which voice adjectives shift?
   - Which content themes are added?
   - How does the relationship type gain nuance?

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Voice Character guide | Adjective lists (is/is not) plus sample sentences | Feeds directly into Tone of Voice framework |
| Visual Style Direction | Mood description, embrace/avoid lists | Feeds into M4 Design Brief and visual identity work |
| Customer Relationship Type | Named dynamic with behavioural descriptions | Feeds into Brand Prism (relationship facet) and customer experience design |
| Content Themes | Themed list with emotional job connections | Feeds into Messaging Architecture and content strategy |

**Validation:**

- [ ] Voice adjectives are **specific** enough to distinguish this brand from a competitor using a different archetype.
- [ ] Sample sentences sound like a **real brand**, not a textbook definition of the archetype.
- [ ] The relationship type is **actionable**: a support agent could read it and know how to respond to an angry customer.
- [ ] Every content theme traces back to a **customer emotional or social job**, not just an archetype cliche.

## Task 5: Validate Against Competitors And Wire Into Brand Artefacts

**Goal:** Confirm that the selected archetype creates genuine differentiation, finalise the Brand Archetypes document, and connect outputs to all downstream brand frameworks.

**Inputs:**

| Input | Source | Required |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Archetype Expression Guide | Task 4 | Yes |
| Competitor archetype map | Task 2 | Yes |
| Brand process doc | `brand_process.md` | Yes |
| M3 founder diary | `projects/[project]/founder/brand/m3_founder_diary.md` | Yes |

**Action:**

1. Perform a **differentiation stress test**:
   - For each of the top 3 competitors, compare their apparent archetype expression (voice, visual style, relationship) to yours.
   - Ask: "If a customer encountered our brand and a competitor's side by side, would they feel a **distinct character difference** in the first 30 seconds?"
   - If the answer is no, revisit Task 4 and sharpen the expression or reconsider the secondary archetype.
2. Perform a **coherence stress test**:
   - Read the Voice Character, Visual Style, Relationship Type, and Content Themes together as a whole.
   - Ask: "Do these feel like they belong to the **same character**, or do they pull in different directions?"
   - If incoherent, identify the outlier element and revise it.
3. Compile the final **Brand Archetypes document** with all outputs from Tasks 1-5:
   - Emotional Territory Brief.
   - Archetype Evaluation Matrix.
   - Primary and secondary archetype selection with justification.
   - Archetype Expression Guide (voice, visuals, relationship, themes).
   - Competitor differentiation analysis.
4. Save the document in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/brand_archetypes.md`.
5. Update the **M3 founder diary** with:
   - The archetype selection decision and rationale.
   - Any trade-offs or risks in the choice.
   - How the archetype connects to M1 emotional/social jobs.
6. Prepare explicit integration notes for downstream frameworks:
   - **Brand Prism**: The archetype personality feeds directly into the Personality facet.
   - **Golden Circle**: The archetype's core motivation should align with the Why.
   - **Tone of Voice**: Voice Character from Task 4 becomes the starting point.
   - **Messaging Architecture**: Content Themes from Task 4 seed the messaging pillars.
   - **M4 Design Brief**: Visual Style Direction from Task 4 informs visual identity.

**Output:**

| Output | Format | Purpose |
|--------|--------|---------|
| Final Brand Archetypes document | Markdown in `projects/[project]/founder/brand/` | Canonical archetype analysis for M3 and downstream use |
| Competitor differentiation map | Section within archetype document | Proves the archetype choice creates market distinctiveness |
| M3 founder diary entry | Row(s) in diary | Captures decision, trade-offs, and rationale for future reference |
| Integration notes | Section within archetype document | Explicit wiring instructions for Brand Prism, Golden Circle, Tone of Voice, Messaging Architecture, and M4 |

**Validation:**

- [ ] A customer encountering your brand and a competitor's would perceive a **distinct character** within 30 seconds.
- [ ] All four expression dimensions (voice, visuals, relationship, themes) feel like they belong to the **same character**.
- [ ] The archetype document is saved and linked from the brand folder.
- [ ] The M3 founder diary contains at least one entry about archetype selection.
- [ ] Integration notes explicitly name what each downstream framework receives from this analysis.

## Pitfalls

**Choosing An Aspirational Archetype Instead Of An Authentic One**

Founders frequently select the archetype they wish their brand embodied rather than the one their customers actually need. A B2B analytics startup choosing the Outlaw because the founder admires disruption, when customers crave the Sage's clarity and the Ruler's control, creates a brand that feels performative.

**Instead:** Ground every archetype score in validated emotional and social jobs from JTBD and M2. If the archetype does not map to a real customer motivation, it does not belong in the top three regardless of how exciting it feels.

---

**Treating Archetype Selection As A Personality Quiz**

Many teams reduce archetype work to a quick workshop exercise: "Which one sounds like us?" This produces shallow results disconnected from customer evidence and competitive reality.

**Instead:** Follow the full evaluation matrix process. Score all twelve archetypes against three dimensions (emotional fit, purpose fit, differentiation). Require written rationale for the top scorers. The rigour is the point.

---

**Blending Too Many Archetypes**

Selecting three or four archetypes to "capture our complexity" dilutes brand character into incoherence. A brand that is simultaneously the Sage, the Jester, and the Caregiver has no recognisable character at all.

**Instead:** Select one primary archetype. Add at most one secondary at a 70/30 blend ratio. If more than two archetypes seem equally valid, your Emotional Territory Brief is too vague. Return to Task 1 and sharpen it.

---

**Skipping Competitive Differentiation**

An archetype that perfectly matches your customer's emotional jobs but is already owned by a dominant competitor will make your brand feel like an imitation rather than an original.

**Instead:** Always complete the competitor archetype map in Task 2. If a dominant competitor already owns your top-scoring archetype, consider whether your secondary archetype could become primary, or whether a distinctive blend creates separation.

---

**Defining Archetype In Theory But Not In Expression**

Declaring "we are the Sage" without specifying how the Sage sounds, looks, relates to customers, and chooses content themes produces a label with no operational value.

**Instead:** Complete Task 4 in full. The Voice Character, Visual Style Direction, Relationship Type, and Content Themes are the deliverables that actually influence downstream work. The archetype label alone is worthless.

## Integration

**Prerequisites:**
Read `brand_process.md`. MUST have completed M1 Conception (JTBD, Working Backwards, Lean Canvas) and M2 Validation.

**Builds On:**

| Framework | Output Used | How Used |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) | Emotional jobs, social jobs, forces analysis | Provides the customer motivations that ground archetype evaluation and selection |
| Working Backwards (PR/FAQ) | Customer narrative, PR tone, customer quote, leader quote | Supplies customer language, aspirational framing, and tonal reference for archetype expression |
| Lean Canvas | UVP, Customer Segments, Unfair Advantage | Aligns archetype purpose fit with business model and value proposition |
| M2 Validation findings | Validated assumptions about customer pain, motivation, and behaviour | Ensures archetype selection is based on confirmed customer reality, not untested hypotheses |

**Feeds Into:**

| Framework | Output Provided | How Used |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Brand Prism (Kapferer) | Primary archetype personality, relationship type, content themes | Personality facet derives directly from archetype; relationship facet uses relationship type; culture facet references archetype values |
| Golden Circle (Sinek) | Archetype core motivation and purpose alignment | The Why should resonate with the archetype's fundamental motivation |
| Tone of Voice Guidelines | Voice Character (is/is not adjectives, sample sentences) | Voice dimensions and examples are seeded by archetype voice character |
| Messaging Architecture | Content themes with emotional job connections | Messaging pillars and narrative themes draw from archetype content territory |
| M4 Design Brief | Visual Style Direction (mood, embrace/avoid) | Visual identity work uses archetype visual direction as a starting constraint |

**Workflow Position:**

Brand Archetypes sits in M3 as Step 2, the first brand framework executed after the brand inputs brief is prepared in Step 1. All subsequent brand frameworks (Brand Prism, Golden Circle, Positioning, Messaging, Tone of Voice) build on the archetype selection.

## For AI Agents

**Execution Context:**

1. Read `brand_process.md` and confirm that M1 (JTBD, Working Backwards, Lean Canvas) and M2 Validation artefacts exist before starting.
2. Track progress explicitly: "Currently in Task [N]: [Task Name]".
3. Confirm each task's validation checklist before proceeding to the next task.
4. When building the Emotional Territory Brief, extract verbatim quotes and job statements from source artefacts rather than paraphrasing from memory.
5. When scoring archetypes, require the user to provide evidence for each score rather than accepting intuitive assessments.
6. Record the archetype decision and rationale in the M3 founder diary as soon as Task 3 is complete.
7. Prepare explicit integration notes for downstream frameworks before marking the framework as complete.

**Working With Tasks:**

- Complete each task atomically. Do not skip the evaluation matrix or attempt to select an archetype without scoring all twelve.
- If the user wants to skip the competitor audit, surface the risk explicitly: archetype selection without differentiation analysis may produce a brand that feels derivative.
- If the Emotional Territory Brief yields no clear emotional or social jobs, flag this as a blocker and recommend returning to JTBD before continuing.
- When the user selects a secondary archetype, verify that the primary-secondary combination does not create incoherence (for example, Outlaw + Ruler, Innocent + Outlaw).
