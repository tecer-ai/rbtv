# Problem-Solution Fit Canvas: Robotville v4.0

## Problem-Space Brief

**Customer Segment:** Aspiring first-time business creators — professionals (25-50) with domain expertise in their field, no startup experience, limited technical sophistication, no formal training in business innovation or strategy.

**Situation:** When they decide to actually pursue a business idea but don't know what steps to take — they have the idea and the motivation, but the methodology to go from "what if" to "validated concept" is inaccessible.

**In scope:** Non-technical professionals with business ideas who lack structured methodology. One segment, one situation: the gap between having an idea and knowing how to develop it.

**Out of scope:**
- Technical founders who can use developer tools (they have Cursor + RBTV already)
- Serial entrepreneurs with prior startup experience (they know the frameworks)
- Corporate innovation teams with budgets for consultants (they have access to methodology)
- Brazil-specific distribution (WhatsApp, Pix) — treated as channel/constraint, not segment

## Problem, Triggers, and Emotions

### Problem Situations

| # | Situation | Context | Consequence |
|---|-----------|---------|-------------|
| 1 | **The bar napkin moment** | Having drinks with friends, describes a business idea with genuine excitement. Friends say "you should do that!" | Idea stays as a story they retell. No next step exists. Excitement fades within days. Idea dies by attrition. |
| 2 | **The blank template wall** | Googles "how to start a business," finds a Lean Canvas PDF or business plan template. Opens it. Stares at the fields. | Closes the tab within 10 minutes. Doesn't know the vocabulary. Feels inadequate. Gives up on structured approach entirely. |
| 3 | **The ChatGPT dead end** | Opens ChatGPT, types "help me with my business idea." Gets a generic list. Asks follow-ups. Gets more lists. No pushback, no structure, no sequence. | Ends up with 2 pages of AI text that sounds smart but doesn't build toward anything. Can't tell if they've made progress or just generated words. |
| 4 | **The vibe coding trap** | The more curious/technical ones open Lovable or similar and start building an app. Skips all business thinking — no customer definition, no competitive analysis, no validation. | Ships something nobody wants. Realizes months later they built the wrong thing. Or never realizes it. |
| 5 | **The consultant sticker shock** | Reaches out to a business consultant or accelerator program. Gets quoted $5K+ or told to apply and wait months. | Decides it's "not serious enough yet" to justify the cost. Goes back to doing nothing. |

### Triggers to Act

**Critical nuance (from Party Mode challenge):** Not all people with business ideas experience pain. Many feel *indifference* — the idea is a pleasant daydream, not an urgent problem. The trigger model must distinguish between the majority who never cross the activation threshold and the minority who do. Robotville's actual addressable segment is people who have *already crossed* from curiosity to intent — a smaller but much more motivated group.

**Internal triggers (for those who cross the threshold):**
- A specific moment of commitment — not accumulated frustration, but a decision point: "I'm actually going to do this"
- Growing curiosity that shifts from passive to active — the idea won't leave them alone, and they start *researching* not just *daydreaming*
- Fear of missing the AI/market window — "if I don't do it now, someone else will"
- Life milestone pressure — turning 30/40, career transition, feeling time running out to take a risk

**External triggers:**
- Career disruption — layoff, restructuring, burnout — forcing a "what's next?" moment (strongest trigger: removes the competing priority of a day job)
- Seeing someone else launch a similar idea (or any idea) successfully — jealousy + proof of possibility
- AI hype cycle making it feel accessible — "AI can do everything now, why can't it help me think?"
- A friend or colleague asking "whatever happened with that business idea?" — social accountability breaking the comfort loop

**What does NOT trigger action (important for positioning):**
- Accumulated frustration alone — most people tolerate inaction indefinitely
- Shame at not knowing — most people don't feel shame about something they haven't committed to
- Generic inspiration from podcasts/books — produces activity (googling, downloading templates) but not sustained engagement

### Emotional States

**Critical nuance (from Party Mode challenge):** The "before" emotions must match where the customer actually is, not where a founder or YC mentor imagines them. Pre-commitment users don't feel imposter syndrome — that requires having entered an arena and felt judged. They feel *curiosity mixed with uncertainty.* The "after" state is not about rigorous thinking — it's about *clarity and permission to act.* The customer wants to feel ready, not analytically exhaustive.

| Phase | Emotional State |
|-------|----------------|
| **Before (current)** | Curious and excited about the idea, but uncertain about what to do next. Not paralyzed — more like *stalled at the starting line.* Vaguely aware they're missing something but can't name it. When they try tools (Google, ChatGPT, templates), they feel a gap between what they expected and what they got — not shame, but mild disappointment and loss of momentum. The idea quietly recedes, not with a bang but with a slow fade. |
| **After (desired)** | Clear on who the customer is, what the problem is, and why their solution makes sense. Able to explain the business to a friend, a partner, or an investor in concrete terms — not business jargon, but real words. Feeling of *permission to act* — "I've done enough thinking, I know enough to take the next step." Sense of real progress, not just activity. Pride in having turned a vague idea into something structured and defensible. |

## Alternatives, Behaviours, and Constraints

### Available Solutions

| Alternative | Drawback for This Segment |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| **ChatGPT / AI chatbots** | Unstructured. No business frameworks. No pushback. Most users treat it as a search engine, not a thinking partner. Produces text that sounds smart but doesn't build toward anything. |
| **Business consultants** | $5K+ minimum. Inaccessible on-demand. Not available at the moment the idea strikes. Overkill for "I have an idea, where do I start?" |
| **AI business platforms / courses** | Often misleading — selling promises without delivering real structured methodology. Generic content, not personalized to the user's idea. |
| **Lovable / vibe coding tools** | Builds without thinking. No customer validation, competitive analysis, or brand strategy. Produces technically functional outputs that may solve no real problem. |
| **Google + blank templates** | Lean Canvas PDFs, blog posts, YouTube tutorials. No guidance, no feedback, no iteration. User stares at a blank template and gives up. |
| **Do nothing (the comfort zone)** | Most common and most dangerous alternative. Inaction is not a failure mode — it's a *competing product* with real benefits: zero risk, social validation from retelling the idea, identity as "the person with the idea" without exposure to failure. The psychological reward of talking about the idea substitutes for building it. Robotville competes not just with bad tools but with the comfort of never committing. |
| **The social validation loop** | Talking to friends, family, colleagues who say "great idea!" This is not neutral — it's an active alternative that delivers emotional satisfaction (belonging, identity, encouragement) without risk or effort. For many, this loop *is* the product. It replaces the need for methodology entirely. |

### Behaviour Patterns

When the problem appears, the customer typically:
1. **Talks to friends** who validate with "great idea!" — feels good, delivers real emotional reward (identity, belonging, encouragement), and actively substitutes for structured development
2. **Googles** "how to start a business" or "business plan template" — gets overwhelmed, closes tabs
3. **Opens ChatGPT** for a few rounds of conversation — gets generic lists, no structure, abandons
4. **Thinks about it endlessly** without acting — the idea becomes a recurring daydream, not a project
5. **Jumps to building** (if more technical) — opens Lovable or hires a freelancer, skips all business thinking
6. **Opens blank templates** (Lean Canvas PDF, Notion template) — fills in 2 fields, realizes they don't know the vocabulary, closes

### Channels of Behaviour

| Channel | Role in Problem |
|---------|----------------|
| **Messaging apps** (WhatsApp, Slack) | Where they communicate daily. Where they'd discuss the idea with friends. Where they are most reachable and comfortable. |
| **Google / YouTube** | Where they search for answers. First stop when they decide to "figure this out." |
| **ChatGPT / AI tools** | Where they try to get AI help. Increasingly the second stop after Google. |
| **Social media** (Instagram, LinkedIn, Twitter) | Where they see others launching businesses, fuelling FOMO and triggering action. |
| **Coffee shops, bars, commutes** | Where ideas actually happen — unstructured moments, mobile-only, away from a desk. |

### Customer Constraints

| Constraint Type | Specifics |
|----------------|-----------|
| **Financial** | No budget for consultants. Hesitant to spend on something "not serious enough yet." Will pay $15-50/month if value is clear, but anything above that triggers "I should be further along before spending this." |
| **Time & Attention** | Business idea is a side project alongside a day job and family. Available in fragments — 20 minutes on the bus, 30 minutes at night. Cannot commit to multi-hour workshop sessions. |
| **Skills & Tools** | No business vocabulary (doesn't know what TAM or UVP means). No experience with structured frameworks. No technical skills — can type a message, cannot use a code editor or fill a spreadsheet model. |
| **Psychological** | Not imposter syndrome (they haven't entered the arena yet) but *uncertainty about whether they belong.* Avoids tools that feel like they're "for real entrepreneurs" or require vocabulary they don't have. Needs an interface that feels like a conversation, not a form. The biggest psychological constraint is not fear — it's that inaction is comfortable and socially rewarded ("great idea!"), so the activation energy to switch from daydreaming to doing is high. |

## Our Solution

### Core Mechanism

Instead of *talking about their idea to friends who validate without challenging*, the customer now talks to an AI mentor through their messaging app — same casual channel, same conversational format, but the response is structured methodology with pushback. The fundamental change: the social validation loop is replaced by a *progress loop.* Each conversation produces a concrete artefact (a framework document), not just a good feeling. The customer gets the emotional reward of being heard AND the functional reward of moving forward.

For digital businesses — the most common use case — Robotville goes further: the same system that structured the thinking also guides the building. After validating the concept (M1-M2), defining the brand (M3), and prototyping (M4), the user doesn't need to leave and find a developer or learn a vibe coding tool. Robotville orchestrates the transition from business innovation to digital product — from validated concept to a live website or app deployed at `robotville.ai/app/{project-name}`. The user never experiences the "now what?" gap between having a plan and having a product.

### Constraints Addressed

| Constraint | How Solution Responds |
|------------|----------------------|
| **Financial** ($15-50 max) | Free during beta, subscription model undercuts consultants by 100x. No commitment before value is experienced. |
| **Fragmented time** (20-30min bursts) | Each framework is a focused conversation. State persists via project-memo — user picks up where they left off. Works on the bus, at night, during lunch. |
| **No business vocabulary** | User never needs to know what "TAM" or "UVP" means. The Mentor translates frameworks into plain questions. Output uses proper terminology but input is natural language. |
| **No technical skills** | User never needs to code, use a design tool, or configure hosting. For digital businesses, Robotville handles the transition from business concept (M1-M3) to built product (M4-M6) — prototype, brand identity, and deployed website/app all generated through the same conversational interface. |
| **Belonging uncertainty** | Interface is a chat message — the most familiar, low-stakes interaction possible. No forms, no dashboards, no "entrepreneur toolkit" branding. Feels like texting someone smart, not entering a business school. |
| **Comfort of inaction** | Solution *respects* this constraint rather than fighting it. Doesn't demand commitment upfront. First interaction is as casual as the bar napkin conversation but produces something the bar napkin never did: a structured next step. |

### New Customer Journey

Same situation: the bar napkin moment. Excitement about an idea for a local food delivery app, friends saying "you should do that!" But now, instead of the excitement fading over the next few days, the customer opens their messaging app on the bus ride home and types "mentor." An AI agent greets them and asks: "Tell me about this idea. Who's the customer?" The conversation feels like talking to a sharp friend who actually pushes back. Twenty minutes later, the customer has answered a series of questions they never would have thought to ask themselves. Their phone has a Working Backwards document — a structured articulation of who the customer is, what the problem is, and what the solution does. They didn't fill out a form. They didn't learn vocabulary. They had a conversation, and the conversation produced something real.

The next day, they pick up where they left off. Over the next two weeks — in 20-30 minute sessions on the bus, during lunch, before bed — the Mentor walks them through customer jobs, competitive landscape, problem-solution fit, lean canvas, assumption mapping, brand identity. Each conversation builds on the last. The idea isn't fading — it's building.

Then the transition that no other tool provides: the same Mentor that challenged their assumptions now guides them to build. The validated concept becomes a prototype. The brand identity becomes a real design. The business model becomes a landing page. By the end of M6, the customer has a live website at `robotville.ai/app/local-food-delivery` — not because they learned to code, but because the system that understood their business was also equipped to build it. They went from a bar napkin to a deployed digital product, and the only interface they ever touched was their messaging app.

### Existential Assumptions

PSF-1 through PSF-3 are existential. If the segment is too small (PSF-1), the first interaction loses to the comfort zone (PSF-2), or nobody finishes (PSF-3) — there is no business. Everything else is solvable with engineering or iteration.

## Synthesis

### Alignment Validation

**Problem-Solution Fit Status:** Validated at concept level — strong internal consistency across all canvas blocks. Three existential assumptions (PSF-1 through PSF-3) require M2 validation before confidence can increase.

### Key Findings

**Segment & Situation:** Aspiring first-time business creators (25-50, domain expertise, no startup experience, non-technical) at the moment they cross from daydreaming to active intent — they've decided to pursue the idea but don't know the steps.

**Problem-Solution Thesis:** The methodology to go from raw idea to validated business concept is locked behind expensive consultants, MBA programs, or tools that require technical sophistication → Robotville replaces the social validation loop ("friends who say great idea") with a progress loop (AI mentor that challenges thinking and produces structured artefacts) through the messaging apps users already live in.

**Core Mechanism:** Instead of talking about their idea to friends who validate without challenging, the customer talks to an AI mentor through chat — same conversational format, but each conversation produces a concrete framework document. For digital businesses, the same system transitions from structured thinking (M1-M3) to guided building (M4-M6), delivering a deployed product without the user ever switching tools or writing code.
