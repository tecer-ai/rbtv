# MECE Framework

MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) is a grouping principle that ensures complete, non-overlapping analysis.

---

## Definition

| Component | Meaning | Test Question |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Mutually Exclusive** | Categories don't overlap | "Does any item belong to multiple categories?" |
| **Collectively Exhaustive** | Categories cover everything | "Is there anything that doesn't fit in any category?" |

---

## Pronunciation

Barbara Minto invented the term and pronounces it as one syllable: "meece" (rhymes with "geese"). The common "mee-cee" is also acceptable.

---

## Why MECE Matters

| Without MECE | With MECE |
|--------------|-----------|
| Overlapping categories cause double-counting | Each item belongs to exactly one category |
| Gaps mean missed considerations | All possibilities are captured |
| Confusion about where things belong | Clear, unambiguous classification |
| Incomplete analysis | Comprehensive coverage |

### For Market Sizing

If you break a market into groups with overlap, you double-count customers and overestimate market size. MECE groups yield accurate estimates.

**Non-MECE Example:** Breaking cell phone market into "women, people over 35, and businesses" — a 38-year-old woman with a work phone falls into all 3 buckets, triple-counting her spend.

### For Case Frameworks

MECE breakdowns enable efficient team allocation. If profitability = Revenue - Costs, and Revenue = Price × Volume, and Costs = Fixed + Variable, you have 4 MECE components. Four consultants can each take one without duplicating effort.

**What doesn't work:** Mixing breakdown approaches. If one person looks at pricing, one at fixed costs, and two at specific business units, there's both overlap and gaps.

---

## Application Pattern

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | Define the whole | "All company revenue" |
| 2 | Create initial categories | "Product revenue, Service revenue" |
| 3 | Test Mutual Exclusivity | "Can any revenue be both Product AND Service?" |
| 4 | Test Collective Exhaustiveness | "Is there revenue that's neither Product NOR Service?" |
| 5 | Refine until both tests pass | Add "Licensing revenue" if discovered |

## Common MECE Structures

### Revenue/Profit Analysis

| Structure | Categories |
|-----------|------------|
| Revenue | Price × Volume |
| Profit | Revenue − Costs |
| Costs | Fixed + Variable |
| Costs | Direct + Indirect |

### Market Analysis

| Structure | Categories |
|-----------|------------|
| Market participants | Customers, Competitors, Company (3Cs) |
| Marketing mix | Product, Price, Place, Promotion (4Ps) |
| Market segments | By geography, demographics, behavior, etc. |

### Problem Diagnosis

| Structure | Categories |
|-----------|------------|
| Cause location | Internal vs External |
| Time dimension | Past, Present, Future |
| Stakeholders | Customers, Employees, Shareholders, Community |

---

## MECE Violations

| Violation Type | Example | Fix |
|----------------|---------|-----|
| **Overlap** | "Young customers" and "Price-sensitive customers" | Use single dimension (age OR price sensitivity) |
| **Gap** | Categorizing by "North, South, East" (missing West) | Complete the set |
| **Mixed dimensions** | "Large companies, tech companies, new companies" | Pick one dimension (size OR industry OR age) |

---

## Practical Tips

1. **Use established frameworks** — Revenue/Cost, 3Cs, 4Ps are already MECE
2. **Single dimension per level** — Don't mix criteria at the same level
3. **Quantifiable when possible** — Numbers can't overlap
4. **Test with edge cases** — What about the unusual scenarios?
5. **Iterate** — First draft rarely perfect; refine until passes both tests
6. **Start with a hypothesis** — Frame as Yes/No question to force MECE thinking
7. **Be careful with definitions** — Clarify boundaries (e.g., how many credits = sophomore?)

### Hypothesis-Driven MECE

Starting with a hypothesis question (answerable Yes/No) naturally leads to MECE frameworks:

| Hypothesis Question | Forces MECE Breakdown |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "Can the client profitably enter this market?" | Financial viability, competitive position, operational capability |
| "Can we cut costs by $X million?" | Fixed costs, variable costs, one-time vs recurring |
| "Should we acquire this company?" | Strategic fit, financial return, integration risk |

## MECE in Problem Trees

Every branch level must be MECE:

```
Why is profit declining?
├── Revenue declining (MECE with Costs increasing)
│   ├── Price decreased
│   └── Volume decreased (MECE: Price and Volume exhaust Revenue drivers)
└── Costs increasing
    ├── Fixed costs increasing
    └── Variable costs increasing (MECE: Fixed and Variable exhaust Costs)
```

## Business Segmentation Examples

Businesses can be segmented in multiple MECE ways. The right segmentation depends on the problem being solved.

| Segmentation | MECE Categories |
|--------------|-----------------|
| By product | iPhones, iPads, Macs, Services, Wearables |
| By customer | Consumer, Business, Government, Education |
| By geography | Americas, Europe, Asia-Pacific |

Each segmentation is MECE within itself, but you cannot mix them at the same level.
