# Meeting Type: Advisory / Board

---

## Discovery Questions

### Context
| Question | What this reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| Who is the advisor/board member? What's their expertise? | How to frame questions to leverage their strengths |
| What's the nature of the relationship — formal advisor, informal mentor, board member? | Formality, expectations, what you owe them |
| How often do you meet? When was the last time? | Continuity — what they already know |

### Relationship History
| Question | What this reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| What did you discuss last time? Any open threads? | Continuity and follow-through signal |
| Have they given advice you didn't follow? | Potential tension to address proactively |
| What do they care most about in your business? | What to lead with |

### Objectives
| Question | What this reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| What specific decisions do you need their input on? | Concrete agenda items |
| What questions do you want their expertise on? | Where their background matters most |
| What progress do you want to show? | Status update priorities |
| What bad news do you need to deliver, if any? | Framing preparation |

### Strategy
| Question | What this reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| What do you want them to DO after this meeting — intro, advice, validation? | Concrete ask preparation |
| Any topics to avoid or defer? | Scoping the conversation |
| What impression do you want to leave? | Competence, growth, honesty calibration |

### Logistics
| Question | What this reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| Date, time, duration, format? | Pacing |

## Output Skeleton

### {Title} — Advisory Cheat Sheet — {Date}

#### Contexto
Table: advisor/board member name, expertise area, relationship type, last meeting date, format, duration.

#### Postura geral
Overall approach — what to project. Balance between showing progress and being honest about challenges. How to use their time efficiently.

#### Relationship Context
What they know, what was discussed last time, any open threads or advice previously given. Whether you followed their prior advice (and how to address it if you didn't).

#### Status Update Structure
Concise progress summary organized by what they care about. Table: area → status → key metric or milestone. Designed for a 5-minute verbal delivery, not a presentation.

#### Decisions Needing Input
Numbered list of specific decisions where their expertise matters. Each with: the decision, your current thinking, what you want from them (validation, alternative perspectives, connections), and the specific question to ask.

#### Questions for Their Expertise
Specific questions that leverage their unique background. Not generic business questions — questions only someone with their experience could answer well.

#### The Ask
If there's a concrete ask (intro, resource, endorsement): what it is, why now, how to frame it. If no ask: explicit note that this is a relationship-maintenance meeting.

#### Bad News Framing
If there's bad news to deliver: what it is, how to frame it (lead with what you learned, not just what went wrong), and what you're doing about it. If no bad news: skip this section.

#### Things NOT to Say
Table: statement to avoid → why. Language that would waste their time, signal lack of preparation, or damage trust.
