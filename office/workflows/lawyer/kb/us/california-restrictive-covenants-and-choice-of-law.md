# California restrictive covenants, outside-work limits, and choice-of-law issues

**Base legal:** California Business and Professions Code §§ 16600, 16600.1, 16600.5; California Labor Code § 925  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. California’s baseline rule

California has one of the strongest anti-non-compete policies in the U.S.

### Business and Professions Code § 16600
The general rule is that, except as otherwise provided by statute, a contract that restrains someone from engaging in a lawful profession, trade, or business is void to that extent.

This is the starting point for reviewing:
- non-competes,
- broad “conflict” clauses,
- post-termination restrictions,
- clauses that effectively require a company’s permission to work elsewhere.

---

## 2. The 2023/2024 statutory reinforcement

### § 16600.1
California made it unlawful for an employer to include a noncompete clause in an employment contract or require an employee to enter one, with limited statutory exceptions.

### § 16600.5
California further stated that an unlawful restraint is void regardless of where and when the contract was signed, and bars attempts to enforce such a void contract.

### Practical meaning
California has moved beyond merely saying “unenforceable.”  
It has also targeted **including** and **attempting to enforce** prohibited restrictions.

---

## 3. Employee context vs business-to-business context

This distinction matters.

### Employment context
California is especially hostile to employee non-competes.  
The leading theme from California law is broad invalidity of employment restraints except narrow statutory exceptions (sale of business, dissolution contexts, etc.).

### Business-to-business context
California Supreme Court case law recognizes more nuance where the restraint is between businesses rather than in the employment context.

### *Ixchel Pharma v. Biogen* (2020)
In a business-to-business setting, the California Supreme Court held that a **rule of reason** can apply to determine validity under § 16600.

### Practical consequence
If the agreement is a true arms-length business/advisor contract, the company may argue it falls into the more nuanced business-to-business zone, not the classic employee zone.

But that does **not** mean every broad restraint is fine. It only means the analysis is not as automatic as in the employee setting.

---

## 4. Why the Horizon clause is still vulnerable

The Horizon agreement goes beyond a narrow direct-competition restriction. It says the advisor may provide consulting/advisory services to third parties only if:
1. they are not competing,
2. they do not create a conflict, and
3. the advisor gives prior written notice with client identity and scope,
4. after which Horizon may object.

From the advisor’s side, this is vulnerable because it:
- can operate as a veto over outside work,
- may force disclosure of third-party confidential information,
- is broader than a simple “no direct competition during the term” clause,
- can chill lawful advisory work beyond what is necessary to protect Horizon.

---

## 5. During-term restrictions vs post-term restrictions

### During the term
A company has a stronger argument for:
- loyalty to current engagement,
- no misuse of confidential information,
- no direct competition while being paid / vested.

### After termination
California is much less friendly to ongoing restraints after the relationship ends, especially if the clause is functionally a non-compete.

This matters because Horizon’s agreement says Section 11 survives termination, but the non-compete sentence itself is not carefully cabined.

---

## 6. Labor Code § 925

If a worker:
- primarily resides in California, and
- primarily works in California,

an employer generally cannot require that person to adjudicate outside California or deprive them of California substantive protections, unless the person was individually represented by counsel in negotiating that term.

### Why this matters
If Horizon later argues she was effectively an employee in California, Labor Code § 925 can become relevant even if the contract labels her an independent contractor.

---

## 7. Practical rewrite from the advisor side

A safer company-protective clause would usually be closer to:

- No direct competitive services **during the term**
- No use or disclosure of Horizon confidential information
- Prompt disclosure only of engagements that the advisor **reasonably believes** pose a material direct conflict
- No duty to disclose third-party confidential information
- No post-termination non-compete
- Passive investment carveout
- Explicit approval of existing known roles

---

## 8. Fair company-side point

The company is not wrong to care about:
- conflicts,
- confidential information,
- direct competition while equity is vesting.

The problem is **scope and mechanism**, not the existence of a legitimate concern.

---

## 9. Bottom-line take

For this agreement:
- a narrowly tailored direct-competition and confidentiality package could be defensible,
- the current disclosure/veto package is broader than necessary,
- the survival structure increases risk,
- California is the main legal pressure point against overreach.
