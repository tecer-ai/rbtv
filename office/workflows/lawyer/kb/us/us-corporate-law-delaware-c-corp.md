# Delaware corporate law — startup C-corp essentials

**Base legal:** Delaware General Corporation Law (DGCL), Title 8, Delaware Code  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. High-level structure

A Delaware corporation is fundamentally **board-managed**. That matters because a promise in a services agreement does not by itself create valid stock or option rights unless the corporate approvals and governing documents line up.

| Topic | Core point |
|---|---|
| Board authority | Corporate powers and business affairs are managed by or under the direction of the board |
| Certificate of incorporation | Controls authorized shares, classes, and often exculpation language |
| Bylaws | Operating rules and governance mechanics |
| Equity plan | Often required for broad option / stock grant programs |
| Board or committee approvals | Usually needed for each grant or for grants under delegated authority |

---

## 2. Issuance of stock

### DGCL § 152 — consideration for stock
The board can authorize the issuance of stock for consideration determined by the board, subject to the certificate of incorporation and Delaware law.

Practical implications:
- The corporation must have **enough authorized shares**.
- The board must approve the issuance or a valid process must exist.
- The consideration must be what Delaware permits and what the company documents authorize.

### Why this matters for the Horizon agreement
If the agreement says the advisor will receive stock or restricted stock, the company must still:
1. have authorized shares available,
2. follow the plan / charter / board process,
3. issue the exact number of shares or percentage in a defensible way.

---

## 3. Options and rights

### DGCL § 157 — rights and options
Delaware expressly allows the board to create and issue rights or options entitling holders to acquire stock, on terms fixed by the board.

Key consequences:
- A services agreement alone is not enough.
- There must be a valid corporate instrument or board approval setting terms.
- If the agreement says “option or restricted stock,” that ambiguity matters because they are not the same instrument.

---

## 4. Why “0.1%” is often under-specified

A promise of “0.1%” can be ambiguous unless the company states:

| Missing term | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Percent of what? | Outstanding shares, issued and outstanding, or fully diluted? |
| Measured when? | Pre-grant, post-grant, or post-financing? |
| What instrument? | Option, restricted stock, RSU, phantom equity? |
| What dilution assumptions? | Future pool increases and financing can dilute dramatically |
| What share count? | A fixed number of shares is usually cleaner |

For startup equity, a better formulation is often:
- exact **number of shares**, or
- exact **fully diluted percentage**, with a defined capitalization methodology.

## 5. Interested-director / conflict transactions

### DGCL § 144
Delaware does **not** automatically invalidate a transaction just because a director or officer has an interest in it. Instead, there are statutory safe-harbor routes, typically including:
- approval by disinterested directors after disclosure,
- approval by disinterested shareholders after disclosure, or
- fairness of the transaction.

Practical point:
If your girlfriend has a role at another company that contracts with Horizon, Delaware law does **not** mean “automatic breach.” It means the company should manage the conflict through disclosure, process, and approval.

---

## 6. Fiduciary overlay

Even when a transaction can fit within § 144, Delaware fiduciary principles still matter:
- loyalty,
- good faith,
- fairness,
- proper conflict process.

That means the safest course is:
1. disclose roles,
2. document approval,
3. keep clean information barriers,
4. avoid dual negotiation roles without explicit consent.

---

## 7. Corporate opportunities

Delaware corporations can, in their certificate of incorporation or via board action allowed by charter, renounce certain corporate opportunities.

Why it matters:
If a person serves multiple companies in overlapping spaces, corporate-opportunity arguments can arise unless boundaries are clear.

Questions to ask:
- Has Horizon renounced any categories of corporate opportunities?
- Does the charter say anything on this?
- Is the advisor expected to source deals, hires, or partnerships that could overlap with another role?

---

## 8. Indemnification and exculpation

### DGCL § 145 — indemnification
Delaware allows indemnification and advancement in many cases for directors, officers, employees, and agents, subject to statutory limits and company documents.

### DGCL § 102(b)(7) — exculpation
The certificate can eliminate certain monetary liability for directors, and now in some cases officers, but not for all duty-of-loyalty or bad-faith claims.

Why this matters here:
An **advisor** is not automatically a director/officer. If Horizon wants the advisor to take meaningful governance risk, ask whether there is:
- indemnification language,
- D&O coverage,
- a separate indemnification agreement,
- advancement rights.

---

## 9. Why Delaware matters less for the restrictive covenant

Delaware corporate law may validate the company’s stock-grant machinery and conflict-approval process.  
It does **not** solve California’s hostility to broad restraints on trade if the agreement chooses California law.

---

## 10. Practical questions for diligence

- How many shares are authorized and outstanding?
- Is there a stock plan in effect?
- Is the plan adopted by the board and, where needed, stockholders?
- Who approves grants: full board or compensation committee?
- Is the 0.1% measured on a fully diluted basis?
- Is the award an option or restricted stock?
- Is there a standard grant form?
- Is there a 409A valuation or other FMV support?
