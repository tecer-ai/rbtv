# Delaware conflicts, fiduciary duties, and corporate opportunities

**Base legal:** DGCL § 144; Delaware fiduciary-duty doctrine; DGCL § 122(17)  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. Why this matters here

Your girlfriend appears to sit near a triangle:
- she is tied to a company that contracts with Horizon,
- she may have a director/advisory/employee role there,
- she is being asked to advise Horizon.

That does **not** automatically make the arrangement illegal or breached on day one.  
But it absolutely creates a **process problem** that should be handled explicitly.

---

## 2. Duty of loyalty basics

Delaware fiduciary law is heavily focused on loyalty:
- fiduciaries should not use their position to advance personal interests unfairly,
- self-interested transactions require careful process,
- undisclosed divided loyalties are dangerous.

### Practical point
If she is actually a director of another company, her duties to that company may be stronger and more formal than her duties as a mere advisor to Horizon.

---

## 3. DGCL § 144 — interested transactions

Section 144 provides routes to keep interested transactions from being automatically void or voidable.

Typical cleansing routes:
1. full disclosure + approval by disinterested directors,
2. full disclosure + approval by disinterested stockholders,
3. fairness of the transaction.

### What this means in practice
A cross-role situation is not solved by silence.  
It is best handled through:
- disclosure,
- board knowledge,
- written approval or acknowledgment,
- recusal where needed,
- clear role boundaries.

---

## 4. Corporate opportunities

### General idea
A fiduciary may not simply appropriate for themselves (or channel elsewhere) a business opportunity that belongs to the corporation.

### DGCL § 122(17)
Delaware allows a corporation, under the right governing document setup, to renounce specified categories of corporate opportunities.

### Why this matters for overlapping roles
If she is expected to source:
- customers,
- hires,
- partnerships,
- acquisitions,
- strategic opportunities,

then overlapping company roles can trigger opportunity-allocation disputes unless there is clarity.

---

## 5. What conflict management should look like here

A strong conflict-management package would do the following:

| Step | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Disclose existing roles in writing | Prevents later “you hid the conflict” arguments |
| Have Horizon expressly approve those roles | Avoids ambiguity on signature day |
| Clarify that she will not advise on both sides of the same negotiation without consent | Reduces divided-loyalty risk |
| Build information barriers | Prevents misuse of confidential information |
| Limit role scope | Helps keep advisory duties from sprawling into conflicted operational control |
| Require recusal from specific matters if needed | Standard governance hygiene |

---

## 6. Why “employee + advisor” is not the same as “director + advisor”

The legal risk increases as the other role becomes more fiduciary / governance-heavy.

| Other role | Typical risk level |
|---|---|
| Employee only | Real confidentiality/conflict concerns, but not necessarily formal fiduciary overlap |
| Outside advisor only | Moderate conflict risk; heavily contract/process-driven |
| Director | Highest risk; formal fiduciary duties likely apply |
| Officer | Also high; operational authority plus fiduciary overlay |

---

## 7. Best drafting fix for this agreement

The contract should not just say “avoid any situation that creates or could reasonably be expected to create a conflict.” That is too vague standing alone.

Better approach:
1. list the known existing roles,
2. state that Horizon acknowledges them,
3. require prompt disclosure of **new material conflicts**,
4. reserve recusal / discussion mechanisms for specific matters,
5. tie the restriction to actual conflict management rather than broad ex ante veto power.

---

## 8. Fair company-side point

Horizon is reasonable to want:
- visibility into overlapping roles,
- protection from divided loyalties,
- assurance against misuse of information.

But from the advisor side, the company should solve that with **specific disclosure and process**, not a vague blanket clause that becomes whatever Horizon later says it is.
