# Worker classification — U.S. federal and California rules for advisors / contractors

**Base legal:** IRS common-law test; FLSA federal standards; California Labor Code § 2775 and related tests  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. Why classification matters for this agreement

Whether someone is truly an **independent contractor / advisor** or is functionally an **employee** changes the legal posture for:
- non-compete analysis,
- wage/hour exposure,
- tax withholding,
- benefits,
- reimbursement,
- labor-code protections,
- forum / choice-of-law issues.

A contract label alone does not settle the issue.

---

## 2. Federal tax: IRS common-law control test

The IRS focuses on the company’s right to control:
- **what** must be done, and
- **how** it must be done.

### Main buckets
| Bucket | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Behavioral control | Who sets methods, scripts, approvals, hours? |
| Financial control | Who bears expenses, opportunity for profit/loss, tools/equipment? |
| Relationship of the parties | Written contract, benefits, permanency, key business function? |

### Advisor-friendly factors
- irregular, strategic involvement,
- no set schedule,
- no employee benefits,
- multiple clients,
- paid by project / retainer rather than salary,
- autonomy in methods.

### Employee-risk factors
- fixed hours,
- close day-to-day supervision,
- required reporting chain,
- company equipment and systems as if staff,
- exclusivity,
- long-term integrated operating role.

---

## 3. Federal labor law: economic reality

For wage-and-hour purposes, federal law also asks whether the worker is economically dependent on the company or really in business for themselves.

Recent federal standards have been in motion:
- the 2024 DOL final rule restored a multi-factor economic-reality approach,
- as of 2026 there is active rulemaking proposing change again.

### Practical lesson
Federal classification law is not static.  
Do not rely on a single outdated memo or label.

---

## 4. California: ABC test and exceptions

California uses a worker-protective framework in many contexts.

### Labor Code § 2775
For many California purposes, a worker is presumed to be an employee unless the hiring entity proves all three parts of the **ABC test**:

| Part | Requirement |
|---|---|
| A | Worker is free from control and direction in performing the work |
| B | Worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity’s business |
| C | Worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business |

If the ABC test does not govern due to an exception, California may apply the older **Borello** multi-factor approach.

### Why advisors can be tricky
A lightweight strategic advisor may fit contractor status more comfortably than an embedded operating executive.  
But once the “advisor” starts acting like an internal executive, classification risk rises.

---

## 5. How this interacts with the Horizon agreement

### Facts that help contractor status
- independent-contractor label,
- no employee benefits,
- no fixed salary,
- advisory / consulting framing,
- ability to advise others,
- no authority to bind the company without authorization.

### Facts that could hurt contractor status
- company veto over other work,
- direct integration into company operations,
- long-term recurring operational management,
- close supervision,
- expectation of exclusivity,
- equity as quasi-compensation tied to ongoing service.

---

## 6. Why this matters for the restrictive covenant review

If she is a true independent contractor:
- the company will argue the contract is a business-to-business restriction.

If she is employee-like:
- California’s anti-restraint policy becomes even more hostile to the non-compete structure.

So classification affects litigation posture even if the text says “independent contractor.”

---

## 7. Practical risk reducers if staying in advisor form

- define services as strategic / advisory, not operational management
- avoid fixed employee-style schedule
- preserve ability to work for others, subject only to narrow direct-conflict limits
- avoid manager-like authority over Horizon staff
- keep deliverables and scope bounded
- do not make the advisor essential to daily ordinary-course operations

---

## 8. Fair company-side point

Not every advisory agreement creates employee risk.  
The problem appears when the facts drift from “strategic advisor” to “embedded executive.”
