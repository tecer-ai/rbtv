# Equity compensation for advisors to a Delaware corporation

**Base legal:** DGCL; SEC Rule 701; IRC §§ 83, 409A, 422; Treasury Reg. § 1.409A-1  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. Main instruments to distinguish

| Instrument | What it is | Main concern |
|---|---|---|
| **Stock option** | Right to buy stock later at a set exercise price | Exercise price, vesting, tax timing, 409A |
| **Restricted stock** | Actual stock issued now but subject to vesting / repurchase or forfeiture | 83(b), valuation, repurchase terms |
| **RSU** | Promise to issue stock later | Deferral / tax timing |
| **Phantom equity / cash bonus formula** | Contractual economic right, not actual stock | Contract-only right, no stockholder status |

The Horizon agreement is problematic because it says the award may be an **option or restricted stock award**. Those are materially different.

---

## 2. Advisors are usually **not** ISO holders

### Incentive stock options (ISOs)
ISOs are generally an employee-only tax category. They are usually **not** available to true non-employee advisors.

### Practical implication
If the advisor is truly a non-employee:
- expect a **nonstatutory stock option (NSO/NQSO)** or restricted stock,
- not an ISO.

That distinction matters for tax treatment and drafting.

---

## 3. Exercise price: what is it, and when is it set?

For an option, the exercise price is usually fixed **on the grant date**, not at some later future valuation date.

The Horizon agreement says the exercise or purchase price per share will equal the fair market value per share on the date of grant, as determined by the board in good faith. That is broadly consistent with standard startup practice, but it still leaves questions open:
- what valuation supports the FMV?
- has the board actually approved the grant?
- is there a current 409A valuation?
- what exactly is the underlying share count?

### Important correction
“1/48th” in a vesting clause is **not** the exercise price.  
It is just the monthly vesting fraction after the cliff.

---

## 4. 409A: why FMV matters so much

### Core risk
If an option is granted with an exercise price below fair market value, it can create serious § 409A problems.

### General startup practice
Private-company options are commonly structured so that:
- the exercise price is never below FMV on the grant date,
- no extra deferral features are added,
- the grant is supported by a defensible valuation process.

### Valuation support
For private stock, a reasonable valuation method is required. An independent appraisal can create a strong presumption of reasonableness if it is recent enough and properly used.

## 5. Rule 701: securities-law path for advisor grants

Rule 701 is the classic federal exemption private companies use for compensatory equity grants.

### Who can qualify
Private companies can often use Rule 701 for grants to:
- employees,
- directors,
- officers,
- consultants,
- advisors,

but only if the services are **bona fide compensatory services** and **not** services in connection with raising capital or promoting / maintaining a market in the issuer’s securities.

### Practical consequence
If the “advisor” is really functioning as a finder, capital raiser, or securities promoter, Rule 701 risk increases.

### Large issuance threshold
If aggregate Rule 701 sales exceed the applicable threshold in a 12-month period (including the $10 million disclosure trigger), additional disclosure obligations can apply.

## 6. Vesting: 4-year with 1-year cliff

The agreement uses a standard startup-style schedule:
- 25% after 1 year,
- then 1/48 monthly thereafter.

That structure is common. The problem is not the vesting schedule itself. The problems are:
- the grant is not yet assured,
- the instrument is unclear,
- the underlying percentage is ambiguous,
- the plan/grant documents are missing.

---

## 7. Restricted stock and 83(b)

If the company grants **restricted stock** (actual stock up front, subject to vesting/forfeiture), the recipient may need to evaluate an **83(b) election**.

### Timing
An 83(b) election must generally be filed within **30 days after the stock transfer**.

### Why it matters
Without an 83(b), tax can be triggered later as the stock vests.  
With an 83(b), tax is front-loaded based on value at transfer, which can be favorable if the current value is low and the company may appreciate.

### But
This is highly tax-sensitive. It depends on:
- grant type,
- actual FMV,
- whether the stock is actually transferred now,
- whether there is repurchase risk,
- the person’s tax profile.

---

## 8. Minimum diligence package for any advisor equity grant

Before accepting an equity grant, ask for:

1. the equity plan,
2. the standard grant agreement,
3. board or committee approval mechanics,
4. the share-count basis for the percentage,
5. current fully diluted capitalization,
6. current 409A or other FMV support,
7. whether the award is NSO or restricted stock,
8. what happens on termination,
9. whether there is any acceleration,
10. tax documentation or explanatory memo.

---

## 9. Horizon-specific reading

### What is normal
- board approval requirement,
- FMV-based exercise price,
- 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff,
- use of standard plan documents.

### What is weak for the advisor
- no cash comp,
- no guaranteed grant,
- “0.1%” ambiguity,
- instrument ambiguity,
- missing plan name,
- missing supporting docs.
