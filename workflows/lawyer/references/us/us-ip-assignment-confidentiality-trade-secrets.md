# U.S. IP assignment, confidentiality, and trade-secret issues for advisor agreements

**Base legal:** U.S. Copyright Act; 35 U.S.C. § 261; DTSA (18 U.S.C. § 1833(b)); contract law  
**Last updated:** 2026-04-17

---

## 1. Why this matters here

The Horizon agreement includes:
- broad confidentiality language,
- return-of-materials language,
- assignment of inventions created in connection with services,
- duty to assist.

That is normal in concept, but the details matter a lot.

---

## 2. Copyright: “work made for hire” is narrower than many startups think

For contractors and advisors, “work made for hire” is not automatically available for everything.

### Two main paths
A work can be “made for hire” only if:
1. it is created by an **employee** within the scope of employment, or
2. it is a specially ordered or commissioned work that fits one of the limited statutory categories **and** there is a signed written agreement saying it is work made for hire.

### Practical implication
For an independent contractor / advisor:
- do **not** assume work-made-for-hire solves ownership,
- use an explicit assignment clause,
- define deliverables and derivative works clearly.

---

## 3. Patent assignment

Patent rights and patent applications are assignable, but assignment must be in writing.

### Drafting consequence
If an advisor may contribute inventions, the contract should:
- contain present-tense assignment language where appropriate,
- cover future cooperation,
- define scope,
- distinguish between company-related inventions and pre-existing inventions.

---

## 4. Trade secrets and DTSA immunity notice

### DTSA immunity
Federal law protects certain confidential disclosures of trade secrets to government officials or attorneys for reporting/investigating suspected legal violations, and in sealed court filings.

### Notice point
A company that enters a contract governing trade secrets or confidential information with employees or contractors should include the DTSA immunity notice if it wants to preserve certain remedies.

### Horizon note
The agreement includes a DTSA section. That is generally a company-favorable but standard inclusion.

---

## 5. Pre-existing IP and general know-how

This is where advisor agreements often overreach.

A good advisor-side contract should distinguish among:

| Category | Should Horizon own it? |
|---|---|
| Deliverables or inventions created specifically in connection with the services | Usually yes, if properly defined |
| Advisor’s pre-existing materials / frameworks / templates | Usually no, except perhaps a limited license if incorporated |
| General know-how, skill, experience, residual memory | Usually not fully assignable in a sensible agreement |
| Work unrelated to the services and created without company confidential info | Usually no |

### Problem with broad assignment language
If the assignment clause is too broad, it can appear to grab:
- pre-existing frameworks,
- generalized methods,
- unrelated side work,
- improvements only loosely connected to the engagement.

---

## 6. Suggested protective carveouts

From the advisor side, request:
- **pre-existing IP schedule** or broad pre-existing-IP carveout,
- carveout for general skills, ideas, and know-how retained in unaided memory,
- ownership limited to inventions / works created **in connection with** and **specifically for** the services,
- narrow duty to assist, with compensation after termination.

---

## 7. Confidentiality clause: what is fine vs what is too much

### Usually fine
- non-use / non-disclosure of confidential info,
- return or destruction of materials,
- notice of compelled disclosure,
- DTSA carveout.

### Needs scrutiny
- definitions broad enough to cover everything under the sun forever,
- disclosure obligations that force breach of third-party confidentiality,
- restrictions that effectively prevent lawful work using generalized expertise.

---

## 8. Why this matters especially with overlapping roles

If she has another active role, the agreement should be explicit that:
- she will not use one company’s confidential information for the other,
- she is not required to disclose third-party confidential information to Horizon,
- her obligations are limited to Horizon information and deliverables properly within scope.

**Practical takeaway**
For a true advisor, the safest company document is not “grab everything.” It is “own what is specifically created for the company, leave pre-existing and general know-how with the advisor, and keep trade-secret boundaries clean.”
