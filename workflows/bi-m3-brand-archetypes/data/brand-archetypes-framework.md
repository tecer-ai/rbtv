# Brand Archetypes Framework

**Purpose:** Define brand character using Carl Jung's 12 universal archetypes, grounded in customer emotional and social jobs from M1/M2 analysis.

---

## The 12 Universal Archetypes

Every brand occupies a character role in the customer's mind. Carl Jung identified twelve universal archetypes that recur across cultures and stories because they map to fundamental human motivations.

### Motivation: Provide Structure

| Archetype | Core Motivation | Customer Need | Brand Examples |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **The Ruler** | Control and stability | Feel in charge, organized, successful | Mercedes-Benz, Rolex, Microsoft |
| **The Caregiver** | Service and protection | Feel nurtured, supported, cared for | Johnson & Johnson, UNICEF, Volvo |
| **The Creator** | Innovation and self-expression | Feel creative, original, imaginative | Apple, Lego, Adobe |

### Motivation: Pursue Independence

| Archetype | Core Motivation | Customer Need | Brand Examples |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **The Innocent** | Safety and optimism | Feel pure, simple, happy | Coca-Cola, Dove, Whole Foods |
| **The Explorer** | Freedom and discovery | Feel adventurous, pioneering, self-directed | Patagonia, Jeep, Red Bull |
| **The Sage** | Understanding and wisdom | Feel informed, knowledgeable, insightful | Google, BBC, TED |

### Motivation: Leave a Mark

| Archetype | Core Motivation | Customer Need | Brand Examples |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **The Hero** | Mastery and achievement | Feel courageous, capable, triumphant | Nike, FedEx, BMW |
| **The Outlaw** | Liberation and revolution | Feel rebellious, unconventional, free | Harley-Davidson, Virgin, Diesel |
| **The Magician** | Transformation and power | Feel transformed, empowered, visionary | Disney, Tesla, Dyson |

### Motivation: Connect with Others

| Archetype | Core Motivation | Customer Need | Brand Examples |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------|
| **The Regular Guy** | Belonging and authenticity | Feel accepted, down-to-earth, relatable | IKEA, Budweiser, Target |
| **The Lover** | Intimacy and passion | Feel attractive, desired, connected | Chanel, Victoria's Secret, Godiva |
| **The Jester** | Enjoyment and play | Feel entertained, amused, light-hearted | M&M's, Old Spice, Ben & Jerry's |

## Archetype Selection Principles

### 1. Ground in Customer Evidence

The archetype must align with customer emotional jobs (how they want to feel) and social jobs (how they want to be perceived) from JTBD analysis. Aspirational choices not backed by evidence feel performative.

### 2. One Primary, One Secondary Maximum

Select one dominant archetype (70-80% of expression). Add at most one secondary for nuance (20-30%). More than two creates incoherence.

### 3. Check Competitive Differentiation

An archetype that matches customer needs but is owned by a dominant competitor will make your brand feel derivative. Always audit competitor positioning.

### 4. Demand Expression, Not Just Labels

Declaring an archetype without defining voice, visuals, relationship type, and content themes produces no operational value.

## Evaluation Scoring

Score each archetype 0-3 across three dimensions:

| Dimension | Score 3 | Score 2 | Score 1 | Score 0 |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Emotional Fit** | Directly embodies primary customer job | Strongly related | Tangentially relevant | Contradicts customer needs |
| **Purpose Fit** | Naturally delivers UVP | Strong but not primary | Partial fit | Forced or contradictory |
| **Differentiation** | No competitor in category | One partial competitor | Crowded space | Dominant competitor owns it |

---

## Expression Dimensions

For the selected archetype, define:

1. **Voice Character** — 3-5 adjectives the brand IS, 3-5 adjectives it is NOT, sample sentences
2. **Visual Style Direction** — Mood, colors, imagery to embrace and avoid
3. **Customer Relationship Type** — Named dynamic (teacher-student, coach-athlete, etc.)
4. **Content Themes** — 4-6 recurring topics, 2-3 topics to avoid

---

## Incoherent Combinations to Avoid

Certain archetype pairings contradict each other:

- **Outlaw + Ruler** — Rebellion vs. establishment
- **Innocent + Outlaw** — Purity vs. rule-breaking
- **Caregiver + Hero** — Selfless service vs. personal triumph (can work with care)
- **Sage + Jester** — Wisdom vs. irreverence (can work with self-deprecating humor)

When in doubt, test: Can the same brand authentically express both in a single customer interaction?

- M1 Conception complete (especially JTBD, Working Backwards, Lean Canvas)

---

## Pitfalls

1. **Aspirational over Authentic** — Choosing what you wish vs. what customers need
2. **Personality Quiz Thinking** — Quick workshop choice without evidence
3. **Blending Too Many** — Diluting character into incoherence
4. **Skipping Competitor Check** — Risking derivative positioning
5. **Label Without Expression** — Archetype name with no operational guidance
