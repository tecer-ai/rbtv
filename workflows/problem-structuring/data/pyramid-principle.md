# Pyramid Principle

The Minto Pyramid Principle is a structured thinking framework for organizing and communicating complex ideas clearly.

---

## Core Concept

Organize thinking into a pyramid structure: single main point at top, supported by related arguments below, each backed by data.

**Three-step approach:**
1. Start with your answer/recommendation
2. Highlight key supporting arguments
3. Provide data backing each argument

---

## Why It Works

People ideally work out thinking by creating pyramids of ideas:
- Group together low-level facts seen as similar
- Draw insight from having seen the similarity
- Form new groupings of related insights

Extended thinking ends in a single pyramid held together by a single thought. Communicating requires guiding the reader down the pyramid.

---

## Key Principles

### 1. Encapsulation

Each box captures/summarizes the main point of all boxes below it.

| Level | Contains |
|-------|----------|
| Top | Main recommendation/conclusion |
| Middle | Key supporting arguments |
| Bottom | Data and evidence |

### 2. Answer First

Start with your recommendation, then explain reasoning. This makes it easier for the audience to follow.

**Without Pyramid:**
> "Our growth is anemic, we've identified the most attractive market, and we're positioned to enter it, so we should enter the health food market."

**With Pyramid:**
> "To double profit, we should enter the health food market because: our growth is anemic, it's the most attractive market, and we are positioned to enter it."

### 3. Flexibility

The pyramid enables adaptive communication:
- Short on time? Present top 2 layers only
- Audience questions one branch? Drill deep there
- Message integrity remains intact regardless of depth

---

## Application Pattern

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Identify the main question/problem |
| 2 | Formulate your answer/recommendation |
| 3 | Identify 2-5 key supporting arguments |
| 4 | For each argument, gather supporting data |
| 5 | Verify MECE at each horizontal level |
| 6 | Present top-down, starting with answer |

---

## Two Directions

| Direction | Purpose | When to Use |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Top-down** | Communicate existing thinking | Presentations, reports, recommendations |
| **Bottom-up** | Synthesize and make sense of data | Analysis, discovery, research |

---

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| Burying the lead | Audience loses attention before reaching conclusion | State answer first |
| Non-MECE groupings | Overlaps confuse; gaps miss critical points | Apply MECE check |
| Too many layers | Complexity overwhelms | Limit to 3-4 layers |
| Data without insight | Raw facts don't connect | Each level must summarize below |
